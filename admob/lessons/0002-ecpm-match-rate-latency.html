eCPM, Match Rate & Latency

Waterfall and bidding aren't fighting over nothing — they're trading three concrete numbers against each other. Learn to read them and mediation stops being folklore.

In Lesson 01 you saw how networks compete: sequentially (waterfall) or all at once (bidding). This lesson is about what they compete over. Every mediation decision — which sources, what order, waterfall or bidding — is really a bet on three numbers: how much you earn per impression, how often a request actually gets an ad, and how long the user waits. Get fluent with these and the rest of the course is bookkeeping.

Recall first. Before reading on — from Lesson 01, in a waterfall, what decides the order sources are called in? (Answer near the end. Retrieving it now, from memory, is what makes it stick.)

1 · eCPM — what an impression is worth

eCPM is effective cost per mille — your estimated earnings for every 1,000 impressions. AdMob computes it by dividing estimated earnings by impressions, then multiplying by 1,000.1

1. Google: eCPM is “the estimated earnings accrued for every 1000 impressions,” found by “dividing estimated earnings by the number of impressions received, then multiplying by 1000.” Reports glossary (R12).

eCPM = (earnings ÷ impressions) × 1000

Google's own worked example: an ad unit earning $180 from 45,000 impressions has an eCPM of ($180 ÷ 45,000) × 1000 = $4.00.2 That single number lets you compare a banner against a rewarded video against a whole app — it normalizes away how much traffic each one has.

2. Example figures are Google's, from the eCPM help documentation (R12). Treat all eCPM values as estimates — they move with geography, demand, and season.
Careful: two different eCPMs

The eCPM you type into a waterfall to rank a source is a prediction. The eCPM in your reports is measured after the fact. Waterfall's weakness (Lesson 01) is exactly the gap between those two. Bidding sidesteps it by letting a network name its real price live — AdMob calls a third party's historical estimate its observed eCPM.3

3. “Observed eCPM: a third-party ad network's estimated average eCPM calculated using historical revenue data.” (R12)

2 · Match rate — did the request get an ad?

A high eCPM is worthless if the slot comes back empty. Match rate is the share of ad requests that a source actually answered with an ad.4

4. “Match rate: the percentage of ad requests that received a response from an ad source.” Match rate (R13). This is what people loosely call fill rate.

A request is not an impression. AdMob splits the journey into two ratios — and a leak in either one is lost money.

AdMob deliberately separates the pipeline into two measured steps, not one:5

MetricFormulaAnswers
Match ratematched requests ÷ requestsDid a network return an ad?
Show rateimpressions ÷ matched requestsDid that returned ad get shown?
5. Match rate = (matched requests / requests) × 100%; show rate = (impressions / matched requests) × 100%. “Not all matched requests result in an impression.” (R12)

Why it matters to you: a low match rate points at demand — no network wanted the slot (thin fill in a region, a mis-set eCPM floor).6 A low show rate points at your code — you loaded an ad but never displayed it, or loaded far too early. Same symptom ("we're not earning"), opposite fixes.

6. Causes of thin fill are catalogued in Common reasons for low match rate (R15).

3 · Latency — the number your users feel

Latency is the time between requesting an ad and showing it. It earns nothing on its own — but it is the one number here the user experiences directly, and your mission is the revenue-vs-retention balance. This is where the two collide.

Recall the mechanism from Lesson 01: a waterfall calls sources one at a time. Each network that fails to fill adds its own round-trip before the next is even asked. A ten-deep waterfall can stack ten sequential timeouts. Bidding runs one parallel round, so its latency is roughly the slowest single bidder — not the sum.

Where revenue and retention actually trade

Chasing eCPM by piling more sources onto a waterfall raises your ceiling — but each extra step adds latency, and a slow interstitial or app-open ad is precisely what makes users bounce. So the three numbers are not independent: pushing eCPM up via a longer waterfall pushes latency up too. Bidding is attractive because it lifts price discovery without paying that latency tax. We'll measure the retention side directly in a later lesson.

The one thing to remember

Three numbers, three questions: eCPM — what's an impression worth? Match rate — did the request get an ad? Latency — how long did the user wait? Every mediation change trades at least two of them against each other; there is no free lift.

Recall answer: a waterfall is ordered by the average eCPM you set — not by what a source will actually pay. (Lesson 01.)
