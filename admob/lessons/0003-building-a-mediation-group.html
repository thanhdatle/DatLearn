Building a Mediation Group

Concepts become a control panel. A mediation group is the box where bidding and waterfall sources are assembled into one line-up — and where the three numbers from Lesson 02 turn into decisions.

You now have the mental model (Lesson 01) and the numbers (Lesson 02). This lesson is the thing that actually holds them: the mediation group. Everything you configure in AdMob's mediation tab happens inside one of these, so knowing its anatomy means the console stops being a maze.

No console needed. You don't need an AdMob account open to follow this — we're learning the shape so it's familiar when you build one for real. Bring your own eCPM numbers later; for now the figures are illustrative.

What a group actually is

A mediation group is a configured set of ad sources scoped to one ad format and a targeting rule — the container where you decide who competes and how.1 That scoping matters: a group is never "all your ads." It's, say, "Interstitial · users in the US · Android." You'll have several groups, each tuned to its own slice.

1. “A mediation group is a configured set of ad sources for a given ad format and targeting.” About mediation groups (R4).

How one request flows through it

This is where Lessons 01 and 02 snap together. For a single interstitial request:2

  1. The bidding sources are called simultaneously — a live auction produces one winning bid.
  2. That winner is dropped into the waterfall at the eCPM it actually bid.
  3. The whole line-up — bidding winner + your manual sources — is sorted by eCPM, high to low.
  4. AdMob walks it top-down until a source fills. First fill wins; the rest are never called.
2. The bidding winner “is then placed in the mediation waterfall alongside your waterfall ad sources” and competes on price (R1, R4). Ordering and tuning of the waterfall portion: Optimize waterfall ad sources (R14).

Bidding decides its price by auction; waterfall decides its price by what you typed. In a hybrid group they're finally judged on the same scale — real dollars.

So a bidding source paying $7.10 live would jump above Network X's hand-set $6.50 and win. But if the best live bid were only $2.00, Network X's $6.50 still leads — provided Network X fills. That "provided it fills" is the waterfall's permanent risk, and why you keep a backfill at the bottom.

Tuning it: the manual/auto choice

For the waterfall sources, you either maintain those eCPM numbers by hand or let AdMob optimize them from observed data.3 Manual gives control and drift; auto-optimization tracks reality but hands over the wheel. Most people start manual to learn the levers, then let AdMob optimize once they trust the numbers.

3. Manual vs. optimized eCPM ordering and its trade-offs are covered in R14. Bidding sources need no eCPM tuning — the auction prices them (Lesson 02, observed eCPM).
Where the platforms diverge

The group itself is built once in the AdMob console — it isn't Android or iOS code. What is per-platform: every non-AdMob source you enable needs its mediation adapter compiled into your app — added via Gradle on Android/Kotlin (R5) and via CocoaPods or SwiftPM on iOS/Swift (R6). Miss an adapter and that source silently never fills on that platform — a classic hidden match-rate leak. We'll wire real adapters up in the SDK lesson.

Don't over-stack

It's tempting to add every network to raise your eCPM ceiling. But each extra waterfall source is another sequential call — more latency (Lesson 02), the retention tax. Prefer moving demand into the parallel bidding side, and keep the waterfall short. A lean group with strong bidding usually beats a deep waterfall on the numbers that matter.

The one thing to remember

A mediation group = one format + one targeting rule + a mix of bidding and waterfall sources. Bidding auctions first, its winner joins the waterfall, everyone is ranked by real eCPM, and AdMob takes the first fill top-down. Build the group in the console; add each source's adapter per platform.
