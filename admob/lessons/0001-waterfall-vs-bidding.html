Waterfall vs. Bidding

The one mental model the rest of mediation hangs from: how do competing ad networks actually get asked to fill a single ad slot?

Every time your app shows an ad, it is holding a tiny auction. You have one slot and several ad networks that might pay to fill it. Mediation is how AdMob decides which network gets asked, and in what order. There are two ways to run that contest — waterfall and bidding — and almost every optimization decision you will ever make comes down to understanding the difference.

Why this first. Your mission is the balance of revenue against retention. Both live here: the contest method sets how much you earn and how long the user waits for an ad to appear.

Waterfall: ask one at a time

In waterfall mediation, AdMob lines your ad sources up in order of the average eCPM you tell it to expect, then calls them one by one. The first source is offered the impression; if it can't fill it, the request falls to the next, and the next — like water spilling down a series of steps.1

1. Google: waterfall “calls ad sources one-by-one based on the average eCPM you set, not what the ad source is willing to pay.” Guide to AdMob Mediation (R1).

The catch is in that italicized phrase. The order is based on a number you configured, not on what a network would actually pay for this user right now. A network that would happily pay more today still waits its turn if its configured eCPM is low. That gap between "expected" and "actual" is money left on the table — and the reason bidding exists.

Bidding: ask everyone at once

In bidding (also called real-time bidding or header bidding), AdMob calls all participating ad sources simultaneously and they compete in a live auction to fill the request. The highest actual bid wins.2 No hand-set eCPM ordering, no waiting in line — each source names its real price for that specific impression.

2. Google: bidding “calls all participating ad sources simultaneously” in a real-time auction, helping “ensure you get the highest revenue for your impressions.” Overview of bidding (R2).

Waterfall trusts the eCPM you predicted. Bidding asks the networks what they'll actually pay — right now, for this user.

Side by side

DimensionWaterfallBidding
How sources are calledSequentially, one at a timeAll at once, in parallel
Order decided byAverage eCPM you setReal-time bids from sources
Pricing accuracyEstimated, can be staleActual, per impression
Manual tuningHigh — you maintain eCPMsLow — auction self-prices
Latency riskGrows with each fallback stepOne parallel round

You don't have to choose: hybrid

AdMob lets you run both in one mediation group. The bidding auction happens first, in real time; its winner is then dropped into the waterfall alongside your manually-ranked sources, and the whole line-up competes.3 In practice this is the common setup — bidding for price discovery, waterfall for networks that don't yet support bidding.

3. “The bidding ad source that wins the auction is then placed in the mediation waterfall alongside your waterfall ad sources.” (R1)
Why this touches retention

A long waterfall means many sequential network calls before an ad shows — each adds delay. A slow interstitial or app-open ad is exactly what makes users bounce. Bidding collapses that into one parallel round, so favoring bidding isn't only a revenue move — it's a UX move. We'll measure this directly in a later lesson.

The one thing to remember

Waterfall = sequential, priced by your eCPM estimate. Bidding = simultaneous, priced by real bids. Hybrid runs both. Everything else in mediation is detail on top of this.
