Lesson 02 · ~10 min · 3 drills

Three Slots, Not a Pool

In DOS2 a turn is a budget you spend however you like. In BG3 it is three separate boxes that do not convert into one another — and there is one box you are about to leave empty, every single round, for the next thirty hours.

Divinity: Original Sin 2 gives you Action Points: four a turn, six in the bank at most, and — the detail that shaped every instinct you have — unused points roll over into the next turn.gamepressure AP is a single fungible currency. You save it, you spend it, you front-load a monster turn. Planning a DOS2 turn is budget allocation.

Baldur's Gate 3 does not have a currency. It has three labelled boxes, each holding exactly one thing, none of them exchangeable, none of them saveable. Planning a BG3 turn is not budgeting. It is filling in a form.

Action

Your one big thing. Attack, cast a levelled spell, Dash, throw something.

One per turn. Cannot be split.

Bonus Action

A second, smaller thing — free, every turn, and it does not exist in DOS2. You will forget it.

One per turn. Use it or lose it.

Movement

A distance pool, roughly 9 m. Spend it in pieces, before and after your Action.

Refreshes fully each turn.

There is a fourth resource, the Reaction, but it fires on other people's turns, so leave it for the end of this lesson. All of them recharge at the start of your turn.wiki Nothing carries over. An unspent bonus action is not banked; it evaporates.

DOS2 — the habit

One pool. 4 AP, cap 6, and leftovers roll into next turn.

Everything costs from the same pot, so you freely trade a big spell for two small ones.

Hoarding AP is correct play — it buys you an alpha strike next round.

BG3 — the rule

Three boxes. 1 Action + 1 Bonus Action + Movement. No conversion between them.wiki

You cannot spend an Action as two Bonus Actions. Ever.

Hoarding is strictly a loss. An unused box is simply gone.

Every turn you do not spend a bonus action, you play the turn at two-thirds strength.

Why you specifically will drop the bonus action

Not because you are careless — because DOS2 has no analogue for it. There is no habit to transfer. Your fingers know "click the big attack, check remaining AP, click again." In BG3 the big attack empties the Action box and the interface moves on, and the Bonus Action box sits there, quietly full, for the entire fight.

1. Build the reflex now, before it costs you: after every Action, before you press End Turn, look at the bonus-action icon. That single habit is worth more than any feat you will pick.

And it is not a scrap of a turn. Larian moved a startling amount of the game's best value into that box. Look at what lives there:

Bonus actionWhat it doesWhy it is enormous
Drink a potionwiki Heal yourself without spending your Action Heal and attack in the same turn. In tabletop this costs your whole Action.
Shovewiki Contested Athletics; pushes a creature away Free every turn. Shoving an enemy off a ledge kills it outright — no HP required.
Jumpwiki Leap a gap; costs 10 ft of movement, no check Escapes melee without triggering an opportunity attack.
Hidewiki Break line of sight and vanish Attacking while hidden gives advantage. Bonus action for Rogues only; an Action for everyone else.
Off-hand attack Swing your second weapon The entire reason dual-wielding is worth anything.
Dip your weaponwiki Stand in fire, dip, add fire damage for several turns Free damage on a slot you were wasting anyway.
The Larian gift

BG3 does not enforce 5e's rule against casting two levelled spells in one turn: "the game does not stop a character from casting a levelled spell with both an action and a bonus action in the same turn."wiki In tabletop this is explicitly forbidden. Here it is simply allowed, and it is one of the largest power gains BG3 hands you for free. Mechanic

Drill 1 — retrieval

You attack with your Action and end your turn without using your bonus action. What happened to it?

The Reaction: a fourth box, on someone else's turn

A Reaction is a response that fires on triggers — including during other creatures' turns.wiki One per turn, recharging each round. The obvious one is the Opportunity Attack: a free swing at an enemy who walks out of your melee range.

2. That opportunity attack is the reason Jump matters. Walking away from a melee enemy gives it a free hit on you. Jumping away does not. This is a Larian-specific escape hatch with no tabletop equivalent, and it is superb.
Go and change this setting now

Reactions are configured in the Reactions tab of your spellbook, and each one can either fire automatically or be set to "Ask" — prompting you for confirmation.wiki

This matters enormously, because a defensive reaction left on automatic will burn itself on the first trivial attack of the round instead of the lethal one that follows. Set the important ones to Ask. Consensus — the setting is a mechanic; "set them to Ask" is my recommendation, and you may reasonably prefer fewer prompts.

Drill 2 — application

You are badly hurt but want to attack this turn. What does BG3 let you do that tabletop D&D does not?

The alpha strike DOS2 told you was impossible

One more re-wiring, and it is the happiest one. DOS2 uses round-robin turn order: your character, then an enemy, then yours, then an enemy — enforced, so that "no matter how high a character's initiative is they can never have two allied turns in a row."divinitywiki DOS2 structurally forbids the alpha strike.

BG3 does not. Initiative is rolled individually, and allies can and do land back-to-back in the order.wiki Better still: if you start a fight from stealth against enemies who have not noticed you, the first round is a surprise round, "during which only the attackers may take actions."wiki

Read that again with DOS2 eyes. A full round of your entire party acting, with the enemy unable to respond at all. The thing DOS2 spent a hundred hours teaching you cannot exist is, in BG3, the single strongest opening in the game. Sneak your party into position before you talk to anyone. Ambushes are not cheesy here; they are the intended play.

Drill 3 — the re-wiring

Why is opening a fight from stealth far stronger in BG3 than in DOS2?

The turn checklist

Run this until it is automatic

1. Action — what is the one big thing? (Usually: control the worst enemy, or hit it.)
2. Bonus action — what am I doing with this? Shove, potion, off-hand, hide, dip. Never leave it empty.
3. Movement — did I end up somewhere I want to be? High ground is +2; standing in fire is not.
4. Reaction — is it available for the enemy's turn?

Two lessons in, you now have both halves of the re-wiring: lead with control, because nothing gates it, and fill all three boxes, because nothing carries over. Everything else in BG3 is detail hung on those two frames.
