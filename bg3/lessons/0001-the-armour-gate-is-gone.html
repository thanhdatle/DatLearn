Lesson 01 · ~10 min · 3 drills

The Armour Gate Is Gone

You have a hundred hours of muscle memory telling you to grind a bar to zero before you try to control anything. In Baldur's Gate 3 that bar does not exist — and the habit will cost you every fight until you delete it.

Divinity: Original Sin 2 taught you a two-phase fight, and taught it so well you no longer notice you are thinking it. Phase one: chew through Physical Armour or Magic Armour. Phase two: the bar hits zero, and now your knockdown, your stun, your freeze land — guaranteed. Crowd control in DOS2 is not a gamble. It is a reward, and the armour bar is the price.

1. DOS2's rule, stated plainly: crowd-control effects "do not work until the relevant armor has been depleted, but once a character's armor is gone, those crowd control abilities have a 100% success rate." PC Gamer Deterministic, and gated. Remember both words — BG3 inverts exactly one of them.

Baldur's Gate 3 runs Dungeons & Dragons 5th Edition, not Larian's own system. And in 5e there is no armour bar at all. There is Armour Class, which sounds like the same thing and is not remotely the same thing.

Armour Class is a difficulty, not a pool

Armour Class (AC) is a single number measuring how hard a creature is to hit with an attack.wiki It does not deplete. You cannot grind it down. Hitting an enemy twenty times leaves its AC exactly where it started. There is no physical bar and no magical bar — there is one AC, and it applies to every incoming attack equally.

And here is the part that should make you sit up: AC does not protect against crowd control at all. Not partially. Not at a reduced rate. It is simply not involved. A creature in full plate with AC 18 is exactly as easy to put to sleep as a naked one.

DOS2 — the habit

Armour gates CC. Burn rounds one and two stripping the bar. Land CC on round three, where it cannot miss.

CC is deterministic but gated. The cost is paid in time.

Splitting damage types is punished — you chew two bars instead of one.

BG3 — the rule

Nothing gates CC. Your hardest control spell is live on turn one, against a completely undamaged enemy.

CC is available but probabilistic. The cost is paid in risk.

One AC, no split. A mixed party carries no structural penalty. Mechanic

Read that right-hand column again, because the conclusion is aggressive and it is correct: the DOS2 opening — soften them up first — is not merely suboptimal in BG3. It is backwards. You are spending your best turn, the one where the enemy is at full strength and most dangerous, doing chip damage, when you could have removed an enemy from the fight entirely.

Lead with the control. Damage is what you do to things that are already helpless.

Two rolls, and they go in opposite directions

Everything in BG3 combat resolves on a twenty-sided die. But there are two different rolls, and confusing them is the root of most first-playthrough bewilderment. The difference is simply who picks up the dice.

2. This is the whole game in one sentence: attacks are rolled by the attacker; saves are rolled by the defender. If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember which direction the die is flying.

An attack roll is you trying to connect. You roll, and you need to meet or beat their AC.wiki This is your sword, your arrow, your Fire Bolt.

d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus ≥ target's AC

e.g. 14 + 3 (STR) + 2 (prof) = 19 vs AC 15 → hit

A natural 20 always hits and doubles your damage dice. A natural 1 always misses, no matter how large your bonuses. wiki

A saving throw is them trying to resist. They roll, against a difficulty you set. This is your Hold Person, your Sleep, your Command — every piece of crowd control in the game.wiki

their d20 + their ability modifier ≥ your spell save DC → they resist

your DC = 8 + proficiency bonus + spellcasting modifier

e.g. 8 + 2 (prof) + 3 (WIS) = DC 13. They roll 9 + 1 = 10 → they fail, your spell lands

Note who benefits from a high roll. On an attack, a high roll is good for you. On a save, a high roll is good for them. The die is the same; the ownership is reversed. wiki

So how do you make control actually land?

You have traded a time cost for a risk cost, and risk can be managed. You have exactly two levers, and a DOS2 player has never had to think about either.

Lever one — raise your DC

Your save DC is 8 + proficiency + spellcasting modifier. Proficiency rises on its own with level.wiki The part you control is the spellcasting modifier — which is precisely why a caster pumps its casting stat to 20 and does not spread points around. Every +1 there makes every control spell you will ever cast harder to resist.

Lever two — target the weak save

This is the real skill, and it is the one that replaces armour-bar-watching. Different spells force different saves, and every creature is bad at some of them. A hulking brute has a monstrous Strength save and a pitiful Wisdom save. A cunning caster is the reverse.

3. The habit to build, and the direct replacement for "which bar do I strip?" — "which save is this thing bad at?" Ask it before every control spell. Hover an enemy and read its stats: the game shows you.

The payoff: Hold Person

Here is why leading with control is not just allowed but devastating. Hold Person is a 2nd-level spell — you will have it in Act 1 — and it forces a Wisdom save. When it lands, the target cannot act. And then this:

Every melee attack against a held creature from within 3 m is an automatic critical hit.wiki

Not "has advantage". Not "is more likely to hit". An automatic critical hit — double damage dice, no attack roll to fail. One successful save-or-suck spell converts your entire party's next turn into guaranteed maximum-value crits, on an enemy that cannot fight back. In DOS2 you would have needed two rounds of setup to earn that. In BG3 it is available on turn one, and the price is that it might miss.

The honest catch

It can miss. A DOS2 player's first reaction to a failed Hold Person is that the spell is unreliable and not worth casting. That is the wrong lesson. The right one: a 55% chance to delete an enemy from the fight is an outstanding trade, and the correct response to a failure is to cast it again — not to retreat to chip damage. Concentration means you keep only one such spell running at a time, which is the subject of a later lesson.

Three things almost every guide gets wrong

Since you are about to go and read other BG3 advice, arm yourself. These three claims are repeated constantly and all three are false. Each one was checked against the datamined wiki.

The folkloreThe actual mechanicWhy you care
"High ground gives advantage" A flat +2 to attack rolls. Low ground is −2.wiki +2 is good, but far weaker than advantage. Do not skip a better play to climb a rock.
"Backstabbing gives advantage" It gives nothing. Absent from the game's advantage list.wiki Stop burning movement to walk around enemies. It buys you literally zero.
"Initiative is a d20 roll" 1d4 + Dexterity.wiki The die is tiny, so DEX dominates. The Alert feat's +5 is bigger than the entire die.
4. I would have told you all three of these wrong from memory. They were caught only by going and reading the datamined wiki. That is the whole reason every claim in this topic carries an evidence badge — and why you should distrust any BG3 guide that carries none.

What to actually do in your first fight

The new opening

1. Before rolling initiative, ask which single enemy would hurt you most — not which is closest.
2. Hover it. Read its stats. Find its weakest save.
3. Open with control aimed at that save, from your highest-DC caster.
4. If it lands, walk your melee up and take the free critical hits.
5. If it fails, cast it again. It was still the right call.

Notice what is not in that list: any step about softening anything up. That step does not exist any more. You spent a hundred hours learning it, and the single most valuable thing you can do this week is stop doing it.
