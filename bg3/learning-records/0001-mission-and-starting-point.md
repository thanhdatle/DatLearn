# Mission set: rewire DOS2 reflexes, don't teach BG3 from zero

Dat is starting his first Baldur's Gate 3 playthrough on **Balanced**, has **not yet created a
character**, and wants to finish the game without getting stuck, savescumming, or living in a
wiki. Spoiler ceiling is **Act 1 only**.

## Established prior knowledge

**He completed Divinity: Original Sin 2.** This is the load-bearing fact of the entire workspace
and it decides what to teach.

- **Transfers intact:** turn-based tactics, action-economy thinking as a *concept*, party
  management, environmental awareness, positioning, the Larian UI, quicksave discipline. None of
  this needs teaching. Teaching it would insult him and waste the zone of proximal development.
- **Actively misfires:** DOS2 and BG3 share a studio but not a ruleset. DOS2 runs Larian's own
  armour/AP system; BG3 runs D&D 5e. The reflexes DOS2 *rewarded* are the reflexes BG3
  *punishes*.

So the zone of proximal development here is **not** "what is a bonus action." It is
**"the thing you have 100 hours of muscle memory doing is now wrong."** That is a much shorter
and much sharper path, and it is the one the lessons take.

## The re-wiring, ranked by how badly each DOS2 habit misfires

Established by research and recorded here because it **is the lesson order**:

1. **The armour gate is gone.** In DOS2, CC is impossible until an armour bar hits zero, then it
   is 100% guaranteed. In BG3 there is no gate — hard CC is live on turn one, resolved by a
   saving throw, and it can miss. DOS2's core loop (*grind the bar, then chain-CC*) is not
   suboptimal in BG3; it is backwards. **Lesson 1.**
2. **Concentration.** DOS2 stacks buffs freely and forever. BG3 permits one concentration effect
   per caster, breakable by damage. The DOS2 opening turn — pile every buff on the frontliner —
   is *structurally impossible*, and a DOS2 player will not notice he is overwriting his own
   spells.
3. **The bonus action.** DOS2's AP is a single fungible pool you can even bank between turns.
   BG3 has three separate, non-convertible slots. A DOS2 player reliably forgets the bonus
   action exists and silently plays a third of every turn down. **Lesson 2.**
4. **The alpha strike is legal now.** DOS2's round-robin order makes back-to-back ally turns
   *impossible by construction*. BG3 uses individual initiative and grants attackers a free
   surprise round. The single strongest opening in BG3 is the one DOS2 taught him cannot exist.

## Implications for future sessions

- **Never teach a mechanic cold. Teach it as a diff against DOS2.** The contrast *is* the
  pedagogy — it is what puts this in his ZPD rather than below it.
- **Do not teach optimisation.** The mission says competence. He is on Balanced and hasn't
  started; a tier list would be both premature and demoralising.
- **Single-class the first run.** Level cap 12 means every multiclass dip costs a real level.
  Out of scope until he asks.

## Correction to my own knowledge (recorded so it does not recur)

Research overturned **three** things I would have taught confidently and wrongly. Recording them
because the pattern matters more than the facts: *game knowledge is folklore-saturated, and my
parametric confidence about it is not calibrated.*

- **Initiative is `1d4 + DEX`, not `d20`.** I would have said d20. Consequence: the die range is
  so small that DEX and Alert (+5 — bigger than the whole die) dominate initiative, which is a
  completely different strategic picture.
- **High Ground is a flat `+2`, not advantage.** Repeated as "advantage" by most casual guides.
- **Backstab/flanking give no advantage** in the released game (an Early Access relic).

**Standing rule: cite bg3.wiki or do not assert.** Every claim in this topic ships with a
`MECHANIC` / `CONSENSUS` / `INFERENCE` badge, and the ones research could not settle — does Prone
really break concentration? — are taught with the hedge visible, not quietly smoothed over.
