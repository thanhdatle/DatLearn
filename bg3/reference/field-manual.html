The Field Manual

Tips, tricks, and the traps a first-time player walks into. Every entry is badged: Mechanic is a datamined fact, Consensus is what good players believe, and Inference is my own reasoning. Nothing here is spoilered past Act 1.

Stop playing scared

You are under-resting. Almost certainly. Mechanic

This is the single most common new-player mistake, and it comes from CRPG paranoia: the game must be punishing me for sleeping. It is not. A long rest costs 40 camp supplies, which are abundant, and there is no global limit, timer, or penalty for resting.wiki

Worse: long resting is how you receive content. Companion scenes, character development, and major story beats fire when you rest. Hoarding supplies means missing the game. Rest whenever you are low. Then rest again.

The honest hedge: a small number of side quests are time-sensitive to rest count. No source enumerates which. The main story cannot be lost this way. Rest freely; just don't leave someone visibly bleeding out for six naps.

You get two free short rests, and you are not using them Mechanic

Twice per long rest you can short rest without travelling to camp. It restores up to half your maximum HP — no dice, no cost, no supplies — plus Warlock spell slots, Ki, Channel Divinity, Action Surge, Second Wind, and weapon-action uses.wiki

DOS2 has no equivalent tier, so you have no habit for it. It is free healing you are throwing away after every fight.

Respec is 100 gold. Build mistakes are not permanent. Mechanic

Withers — found in a sarcophagus in the Dank Crypt in Act 1, and thereafter a permanent camp follower — will rebuild any character from level 1 for 100 gold: class, subclass, ability scores, skills, spells, feats.wiki He also revives dead companions for 200g.

That is trivial money by mid-Act 1. Pick the class that sounds fun. If it does not click, it costs you a pint's worth of gold to change your mind.

Use your consumables. All of them. Consensus

Most players reach the end of the game with a bag full of scrolls, potions, elixirs and throwables they were "saving for a hard fight." This is the hard fight. There is always more loot. A scroll unused is a scroll wasted.

Free value you are leaving on the floor

Shove things off ledges Mechanic

Shove is a bonus action in BG3 — not part of the attack action as in tabletop — and it is available to every character, every turn.wiki It is a contested Athletics check.

Fall damage does not care about hit points, armour, or how legendary a monster is. A shove into a chasm removes an enemy from existence for zero resources. Fight near ledges on purpose. Inference — that the game intends this is my read, but the mechanics unambiguously permit it.

Jump away instead of walking away Mechanic

Walking out of an enemy's melee range gives it a free opportunity attack on you.wiki Jumping — a bonus action costing 10 ft of movement, no check requiredwiki — does not.

Your squishy caster should almost never walk out of melee.

Potions are a bonus action. Heal and attack in one turn. Mechanic

"Consuming a potion only costs a bonus action."wiki In tabletop it eats your whole Action. Here, healing is nearly free — it costs a box you were probably leaving empty anyway.

Better: potions can be thrown, shattering to heal everyone nearby. That is an AoE heal from a Healing Potion. Consensus

Every magic item works the moment you equip it Mechanic

"There is no magic item attunement system. All magic items can be equipped immediately."wiki Tabletop limits you to three attuned items. BG3 removes the limit entirely.

So actually read your loot. A pile of minor +1 trinkets stacks into a real power spike, and nothing stops you wearing all of it at once.

Weapon actions, once per short rest Mechanic

Every weapon type grants special weapon actions — usable once each per short rest.wiki They are not a tabletop concept and there is no prompt telling you they exist. Most players never press them.

They cost a mix of Action, Bonus Action, and Movement. Look at your hotbar. They are right there.

Everyone casts rituals; nobody needs components Mechanic

"All caster classes are capable of ritual casting" at no extra time cost (out of combat), and verbal, somatic and material components are ignored entirely — no focus needed, and you can cast with both hands full.wiki

Free out-of-combat utility spells that cost you no slot. Cast them liberally.

Traps a first-timer walks into

Armour proficiency is brutal — check before you equip Mechanic

Wearing armour you are not proficient with gives disadvantage on every ability check, saving throw, and attack roll — and you cannot cast spells at all.wiki

Companions will happily equip anything you hand them. Put plate on your Wizard and you have silently disabled your Wizard. This is the most common accidental self-sabotage in the game.

A shield does nothing while you hold a bow Consensus

Swapping to a ranged weapon unequips your shield, and with it the AC it was giving you. If you shoot and then end your turn, you spend the enemy's entire round at lower AC. Swap back to melee before ending your turn.

Heavy armour has no Strength requirement here Mechanic

Larian did not implement 5e's Strength requirement or movement penalty on heavy armour. An 8-Strength character in full plate moves exactly as fast as an 18-Strength one.wiki

If you know tabletop, this will feel wrong. It is not. Heavy armour also gives no Dexterity bonus at all and imposes disadvantage on Stealth — those are the real costs.

Dump stats are free, and 8 is a fine number Mechanic

Point buy gives you 27 points, scores run 8–15 before racial bonuses, and 8 costs zero points.wiki Two bonuses (+2/+1) then apply, so you can start at 17.

Spreading points evenly is the classic first-timer error. Push your primary stat to 17 and dump what you do not use. The only real cost of low Strength is carry weight. Hard cap is 20, reached via feats.wiki

Set your reactions to "Ask" Consensus

Reactions live in the Reactions tab of the spellbook and each can fire automatically or prompt you.wiki On automatic, a defensive reaction burns itself on the first trivial hit of the round instead of the lethal one. The setting is a mechanic; "set them to Ask" is my recommendation and you may reasonably prefer fewer prompts.

Bring a face Consensus

Dialogue checks are real d20 rolls you can fail, and failing them closes content. Pick one character with high Charisma and Persuasion proficiency, and always let them lead conversations. In DOS2 this mattered far less.

Character creation, in ninety seconds

Single-class your first run Consensus

The level cap is 12wiki, so every level you dip into a second class is a level your main class never gets — including its capstone. Multiclassing is powerful in expert hands and a reliable way to build a character that is mediocre at two things otherwise. Not on run one.

Feats come at 4, 8, and 12 Mechanic

Every class picks a feat at levels 4, 8 and 12; Fighters get a bonus one at 6 and Rogues at 10.wiki With a cap of 12, that is three feats for most characters — so each one is a real decision.

Ability Improvement (+2 to one stat, max 20) is itself a feat, and taking it to round your primary stat to an even number is never wrong. That is the safe default when nothing else excites you.

Forgiving first classes Consensus

Fighter — the simplest class in the game; hits things, hard, reliably. Warlock — Eldritch Blast scales on its own, spell slots refill on a short rest, and high Charisma doubles as your party face. Cleric — durable, heals, and Clerics pick their subclass at level 1 so you know who you are immediately.

No "trap class" list appears here on purpose. I could not find a single trustworthy source that labels any class a beginner trap, and any guide that hands you one is inventing it. Play what sounds fun; Withers costs 100 gold.
