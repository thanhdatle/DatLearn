The Only Number That Matters

Before you open Ads Manager, before you pick a geo, before you cut a single video — there is one number you must be able to compute cold: the most you are allowed to pay for an install. Everything else in this course is downstream of it.

Your app makes money one way: somebody opens it, sees an ad, and AdMob pays you a fraction of a cent. That is the entire revenue model. So a user is worth exactly the ads they will ever see — no more — and paid user acquisition is not marketing. It is arbitrage: you buy a stranger for a known price and hope they generate more than that in ad impressions before they stop opening the app.

The whole game is whether the second number beats the first. Most of the time, for an ads-only app, it doesn't. Your own strategy document already reached that conclusion — ads-only LTV is roughly 20–40% of CPI in every tier.1 This lesson teaches you to derive that verdict yourself, for any geo, in about ninety seconds — because a number you can recompute is a number you can defend, and a number you inherit is a number you will quietly ignore the first time a campaign looks exciting.

1. AndroidEmulator/docs/ua-strategy.md, "Scenario summary" (R15). Adopted 2026-07-07. Its figures are ±50% priors from category benchmarks, not measured data — which is exactly why you need the machine, not the answer.

The chain, in four links

Every LTV number you will ever see is this chain. There is no fifth link and no magic.

ARPDAU = ad revenue per active user, per day they open the app
Active days = 1 + r(1) + r(2) + … + r(N)  ← the retention curve, summed
LTV(N) = ARPDAU × Active days
Max CPI = LTV(N) ÷ your margin ratio N is your payback window — the day by which you insist on having your money back.

Link two is the one people get wrong. Retention is not "40% of users stay." It is a curve, and what you need is the area under it — the total number of days an average install will be active. A D1 of 40%, D7 of 15% and D30 of 6% is a decent emulator prior,2 and summing that curve out to day 90 gives about 7.2 active days per install. Not 90. Not 30. Seven.

2. Retention curve prior from ua-strategy.md's LTV model (R15), sanity-checked against AppAgent's genre benchmarks (R14). The calculator below fits a power law r(t) = A·t−b through your three measured points — the standard shape for app retention: steep early decay, long shallow tail. It reproduces the strategy doc's own "~7.5 cumulative active days" independently, which is a good sign that both are describing the same reality.

That single number — 7.2 — is why ads-only apps struggle. Seven days of someone opening your app, at a fraction of a cent per day, is not much of a budget to buy them with.

Sit with the default case for a moment. Tier-3, realistic CPI of $0.25, and you get back a small fraction of every dollar. Now try the optimistic Tier-1 case: ARPDAU 0.09, CPI 2.50. It does not save you. Tier-1 users are worth ~10× more and cost ~10× more — the ratio barely moves. That symmetry is the single most important intuition in this lesson, and it is why "let's just target the US" is not a strategy.

The lever that actually moves it is retention, not geo. Set D1 to 55, D7 to 28, D30 to 15 — a genuinely sticky library habit — and watch the ceiling move. Geo changes both sides of the fraction. Retention changes only the numerator. That is where the leverage lives, and it is a product problem, not a media-buying one.

Why this ceiling is the thing you will be tempted to ignore

Here is the trap, and it is the specific way ads-only apps lose money. When you launch, the only signal flowing back to Meta is installs. So you optimise for installs, and Meta — which is extremely good at its job — goes and finds you the people most likely to tap "Install." Those people are not the people most likely to open your app on day 30. They are cheap, they are plentiful, and they are worthless.

The fix is to make revenue itself the signal: AdMob exposes per-impression revenue through OnPaidEventListener, which hands you getValueMicros(), getCurrencyCode() and a getPrecisionType(),3 and an MMP forwards that back to the ad network as the value it should optimise toward. That is the whole point of Phase 0, and it is why Phase 0 comes before spending.

3. Impression-level ad revenue, Google (R1). Careful: test impressions from bidding sources return precision UNKNOWN and a value of 0 — a test device can never prove your revenue plumbing works. Only production traffic can.

So the first weeks of spend are not for profit. They are for measurement. Which makes the ceiling above the only thing standing between you and an expensive education.

The Phase 1 gate — write these down before you spend

Your strategy doc already committed to three thresholds. After the calibration spend, you proceed only if all three hold:

  • D1 ≥ 35%
  • D7 ≥ 12–15%
  • D7 cumulative ad revenue ≥ 25–30% of CPI

Below any of them, the instruction is to stop and fix retention or ad density before spending more. In the doc's own words: UA scales an economy, it doesn't create one. The D7 ROAS output in the calculator above is exactly the third gate — watch it as you change inputs.

Drills

Drill 1 of 3

Tier-3 gives you $0.008 ARPDAU at a $0.25 CPI. Tier-1 gives you $0.09 ARPDAU at a $2.50 CPI. Which is the better buy?

Drill 2 of 3

You launch with no MMP and optimise the campaign for installs. What has Meta actually been instructed to find for you?

Drill 3 of 3 · the one that matters

Your ceiling is far below market CPI. Which change raises the ceiling without also raising what you pay?

Do this now

This lesson is not finished when you understand it. It is finished when the number exists.

  1. Open the calculator above and set the margin ratio you will actually hold yourself to. 3× is the conventional floor for sustainable growth;5 for a first calibration you may accept less, but decide the number now, while no money is at stake.
  2. Compute the ceiling for each of the five Phase 1 geos — BR, MX, ID, PH, VN — using your strategy doc's ARPDAU priors and its $0.15–0.40 CPI band. Write the five numbers down.
  3. Put them somewhere you will see them when a campaign is doing well and you are tempted. That list is your bid ceiling, and its only job is to be obeyed on a good day.
