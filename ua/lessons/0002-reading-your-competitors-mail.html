Reading Your Competitors' Mail

Every ad your competitors are running right now is public, free, and legal to study. You can't see their spend or their ROAS — but you can see which creatives they've been paying for since March, and that turns out to be the more useful number.

Lesson 01 left you with a bid ceiling and an uncomfortable conclusion: at baseline assumptions, paid UA doesn't pay back. The way out is not a better bid — it's a better creative, because creative is the one input that moves CPI by multiples rather than percentages. And you are about to start cold, with no creative data of your own.

Except you don't have to start cold. Competitors have already spent real money discovering which hooks work in this exact category, and three public archives will show you the survivors. This lesson is the only one in the course you can complete tonight: no SDK, no MMP, no ADR-0004 amendment, no spend.

The three archives, and what each will actually give you

ArchiveCoversGives youDoesn't
Meta Ad Library Facebook + Instagram, worldwide Every currently active ad: creative, copy, start date, platforms, version count Any performance metric — no impressions, clicks, CTR or spend1
TikTok Creative Center Global, but curated Top-performing ads by industry and region, with some reported metrics Anything below its engagement threshold — new or weak ads simply aren't indexed
Google Ads Transparency Center Search, YouTube, Display, Play Verified advertiser, creatives, formats, run dates, up to 13 months back — no login Non-verified advertisers; performance data

Google's is the one most people forget, and for an Android-only app it is the most important of the three: it is the only window onto UAC and YouTube, the channel Meta and TikTok structurally cannot show you and the one that reaches people literally searching "gba emulator".

1. Meta publishes no performance data for commercial ads — Ad Library tools, Meta Transparency Center (R7). This is not a gap in your research skills; the number does not exist publicly. Everything below is about inferring performance from the data that is published.

The trap that catches everyone once. For commercial ads, Meta's Ad Library shows only what is active right now — Meta's own documentation says non-political ads are displayed in the Ad Library while active and are not retained after they stop running. Only political and social-issue ads get the seven-year archive. So a paused ad vanishes, along with the evidence that it ever ran. The library is a live snapshot, not a history book, and that single fact dictates the working method below: you must sample it on a cadence, or you are only ever seeing this week.

The signal you actually have: longevity

You cannot see spend. You can see how long a creative has survived — and that is a genuine performance signal, because ad platforms starve losing creatives of delivery within a couple of weeks, and no one keeps paying for an ad that loses. The convention among media buyers is roughly:2

  • Under 14 days — noise. It may be dead by Friday.
  • 30+ days — it cleared whatever internal bar that advertiser holds.
  • 60+ days — a proven winner. Study it properly.

Two secondary signals sharpen it. Variant count — Meta shows when one ad has multiple versions — tells you they're actively iterating, which means they're protecting a winner rather than leaving an old ad switched on. And geo spread tells you the angle survives translation, which is rarer than it sounds and matters enormously to you, since your Phase 1 geos span five countries and three languages.

2. Longevity-as-proxy is practitioner convention rather than published research — see competitive creative analysis and identifying a competitor's best format (R18). Treat the day-count thresholds as rules of thumb, not physics. The underlying logic — advertisers stop paying for losers — is sound; the exact cut-offs are folklore.

The EU backdoor

Here is the trick that most people running BR/ID/PH campaigns never discover. Under the Digital Services Act, ads delivered to the EU carry obligations the rest of the world doesn't — and Meta keeps EU-delivered ads accessible for one year after their last impression, with targeting parameters and demographic reach breakdowns attached. The same asymmetry runs through the other platforms: Meta's Ad Library API only returns ordinary commercial ads when you point it at EU or UK countries,3 and TikTok's genuinely comprehensive archive — the Commercial Content Library — covers the EEA, Switzerland and the UK only.

3. The Ad Library API is scoped to political and social-issue ads globally, plus all ad types for EU/UK. The ad_type=ALL parameter returns ordinary product ads only when ad_reached_countries is an EU member state or the UK (R19). For everything else, the visual library is the tool — which is why the routine below is manual and time-boxed rather than automated.

So even though you will never advertise in Germany, look at your competitors' German ads. That is where the history lives, where the audience data lives, and where the only programmatic access lives. A competitor who runs the same creative in DE and BR has just told you, for free, who they think it appeals to.

The IP trap — read this twice. You are about to open Meta Ad Library and see emulator apps running Pokémon and Mario gameplay. Every instinct will say: they're doing it, it's clearly allowed, and it's clearly working. It is none of those things. You are looking at survivorship bias in its purest form — the library shows ads that are running right now, so by construction you cannot see the accounts that were already banned for exactly this. TikTok frame-scans creatives for copyrighted content and escalates from rejection to account suspension (R6). Your own strategy doc names this as the thing that kills emulator advertisers. Study their structure — the hook, the pacing, the promise — and rebuild it with µCity, Lan Master and Alter Ego. Never their footage.

The routine — twenty minutes, once a week

Because the Meta library is a live snapshot, this only works as a habit. A single session tells you what is running today; six weekly sessions tell you what survives, which is the thing you actually want to know.

  1. Fix your target list. Start with Gamu — your own strategy doc already treats it as the reference point for the ROM link-out direction — plus RetroArch and two or three of the "Retro Games / Super Emulator" Play listings. Five advertisers is plenty.
  2. Meta Ad Library → search the advertiser → set country to each of BR, ID, PH, and then DE. Record every ad's start date, format, hook, and version count.
  3. Google Ads Transparency Center → same advertisers → 30-day window, then stretch it to the full 13 months. This is your only view of their UAC and YouTube activity.
  4. TikTok → Creative Center for the category's winning hook patterns; the Commercial Content Library for a specific competitor's complete EU history.
  5. Triage each ad with the calculator above, and keep only what scores. Then write down, for the survivors, the one thing that matters: what is the promise in the first two seconds?

Everything lands in one file — the teardown sheet, which is built to be printed and filled in by hand or copied into a spreadsheet. The dated rows are the point: they are how a snapshot becomes a history.

Do this now

Twenty minutes, tonight, and you will have more category-specific creative evidence than your strategy document currently contains.

  1. Open Meta Ad Library, set country to Brazil, and search Gamu. Then repeat with country set to Germany and note what extra information appears.
  2. Run every ad you find through the triage calculator above. Keep only the ones that clear 30 days.
  3. Fill in the first five rows of the teardown sheet — and date the entry, because next week's snapshot is what makes it evidence rather than a screenshot.
  4. Put a recurring 20-minute slot in your calendar. Same day each week. Six weeks of this is a genuine creative strategy; one session is trivia.
