DatLearn/ Strength/ Session 2

Session S2 · Tuesday · ~55 min

Upper — Bench Focus

Horizontal push and pull, balanced set for set. Your legs get a full day off while your chest and back do the work.

Yesterday you squatted. Today nothing below your waist is asked to do anything, which is the entire reason an upper/lower split exists: you train five days a week while each muscle still gets its rest. Frequency is not the magic. Distributing volume without piling fatigue into one place is.

No bike today. The five-minute cycle is a lower-day habit for your knee. On upper days you ramp straight into the bench.

Warm up

Three or four ramping sets on the bench: empty bar, then roughly 50%, 70%, and 85% of your working weight, for progressively fewer reps. Nothing else needs its own warm-up.

Why this order

Bench first, for the same reason the squat leads Monday. Then the row: it opposes the bench, so your chest recovers while your back works, and the set-for-set balance is deliberate. A programme that presses more than it pulls builds a shoulder problem on a schedule.

The pairs that follow are antagonists too — press against pulldown, lateral raise against face pull. Every superset here puts a pushing muscle opposite a pulling one, which is what makes 60–90 seconds of rest defensible rather than merely fast.

Today's calibration test — week one only

On the last set of the lat pulldown

Before you begin the set, write down how many reps you predict you can do to true failure. Then do them — to actual failure, where a rep stalls despite full effort. Compare the two numbers.

Most intermediates undershoot by two to four reps. If you predicted 10 and hit 14, you were nowhere near failure when you thought you were — meaning every "RPE 8" you have logged was really an RPE 6, and the progression rule has been running on a sensor that reads low.

This is a machine. No spotter, nothing to drop. That is precisely why the test lives here and never on the bench, the squat, or the deadlift.

The one thing to get right today

Repeatability is the technique

Same grip, same touch point, same bar path, every rep of every set. Barbell Medicine's central warning about the bench is not about arch or elbow angle — it is about chasing a textbook style that fights your own anatomy. Find the path your shoulders like, then make it boringly identical for two years.

The one genuine error: elbows flaring to 90° while the bar drifts toward your face. That is where benching hurts shoulders.

Check yourself

In week one, where are you permitted to train to true failure?

Log it before you leave

The bench load, the reps on all four sets, and the last-set RPE. In week one, add the two calibration numbers: predicted reps, and actual reps. That pair tells us more about your training than any weight on the sheet.
