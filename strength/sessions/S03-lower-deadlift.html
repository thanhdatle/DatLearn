Lower — Deadlift Focus

The heaviest session of the week, and the only one with a written list of reasons to abandon it.

Wednesday sits in the middle of the week for a reason. Your lower back did squats and Romanian deadlifts on Monday; it gets Tuesday off entirely, because Tuesday pulls from a chest-supported machine rather than a barbell row. By Wednesday your erectors have had 48 hours. That is the whole architecture of this split, and it is why you should not shuffle the days around without thinking it through.

Bike first. Lower day — five easy minutes of cycling before you touch the bar, then ramp the deadlift.

Deadlift cap: RPE 7 in week one, RPE 8 in week two. Reset every rep — no touch-and-go at this intensity.

Why this order

Deadlift first, while your grip, your erectors, and your attention are all intact. The front squat follows because its upright torso spares the lower back you have just taxed — that is why it is here and not a back squat. The split squat and leg curl are supersetted, one knee-dominant and one hip-dominant, so neither steals from the other.

Hanging leg raises last: your abs braced the deadlift, and fatiguing them earlier would have cost you the lift.

Two knee-driven swaps you should understand

Front squat, not hack squat

The original programme let you pick either. I removed the hack squat because it pins your torso and drives the knees forward under load. Be clear about the status of that reasoning: it is my judgement, not a finding. No study compares them for knee health. If the front squat irritates your knee and the hack squat doesn't, believe your knee, not my page.

Bulgarian split squat: the long stride, and why it works

Plant the front foot far enough forward that your shin is close to vertical at the bottom, and let the torso lean slightly forward. This is one of the few knee cues in this whole course with real numbers behind it: increasing step length from 50% to 120% of leg length raised the peak hip-extension moment by 15.7% and dropped the peak knee-extension moment by 15.1%.

It moves load off the knee. It does not abolish it. If the knee still objects, swap to a B-stance Romanian deadlift — nearly all the glute, almost no knee flexion.

The guardrails

Cut the deadlift when any of these is true

Weekly heavy pulling is fine for an intermediate with tripwires. Drop to 2–3 sets, swap to trap-bar or higher-rep RDL, or deload 10–15% if:

· form degrades on the last set — the back rounds, the bar drifts forward; or
· Monday's low-back fatigue is still with you today; or
· the last set exceeds the RPE cap two weeks running; or
· there is any pain at all, anywhere.

Backing off is not failure. It is the mechanism by which people are still deadlifting at fifty.

On "never round your lower back"

The honest version: a systematic review found only low-quality evidence, and it did not support lumbar flexion during lifting as a cause of back pain. The blanket prohibition is not proven. Brace to a near-neutral spine anyway — it is the most efficient way to move a heavy bar, and it buys you margin. Treat it as a skill you are practising, not a law you are obeying in fear.

Check yourself

It's Wednesday. Monday's squats and RDLs have left your lower back still tired. Nothing hurts. What do you do?

Log it before you leave

The deadlift load, the reps on all four sets, the last-set RPE, and your knee out of ten. Add a word about your lower back if it had anything to say — that is the input to next Wednesday's guardrail check.
