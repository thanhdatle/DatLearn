Double Progression & Reps in Reserve

You already have the programme. It isn't the programme that makes you stronger — it's what you decide to do with the bar next session.

You said the thing that stalls you is not knowing how to progress: you add weight until you miss reps, then guess. That guess is the whole problem. Guessing high, week after week, is how a lifter grinds themselves into an injury — and your mission is to still be lifting in twenty years. So this lesson gives you a written rule that answers one question, every session, without a feeling involved: what do I do with the bar next time?

Why this first. Everything else — volume, exercise choice, deloads — is downstream of this decision. Get the rule, and the five-day split starts running itself.

The unit is the hard set

Forget "sets" for a moment. The thing that drives adaptation is a hard set: one taken close enough to failure to matter. To make "close enough" precise, count the reps you could still have done — your reps in reserve (RIR).

Lifters more often speak in RPE, rate of perceived exertion. The two are the same idea, inverted: RPE = 10 − RIR.1

1. Barbell Medicine's mapping: each RPE point is one more rep in the tank; a load change of roughly 5% moves RPE by about 1. Autoregulation & RPE, Part I (R4).
RPEReps in reserveWhat it feels like
100No reps left; another good-form rep would fail.
91One more, maybe. Not two.
82Two clean reps left. Speed still good.
73Three left. Clearly submaximal.

Counting hard sets is a defensible way to measure training, not just a convenience — total hard sets holds up as a volume metric for sets in roughly the 6–20+ rep range.2 Note the honest catch: your main lifts sit at 4–6 reps, just below that validated band. We still count them, because they are hard hypertrophic work — but the weekly audit is an approximation, not a measurement the literature has blessed.

2. Baz-Valle et al., J Strength Cond Res 2021 — total number of sets as a volume quantification method (R12).

You do not have to reach failure

This is the part that protects you. A 2023 meta-analysis found that training to momentary failure was not superior to stopping short of it for muscle growth (effect size 0.12, p = 0.343).3 The extra pain bought nothing measurable — while costing fatigue, recovery, and, on a heavy squat, safety.

3. Refalo et al., Sports Medicine 2023 — proximity-to-failure and hypertrophy (R9).
Where I'm not certain — and you shouldn't be either

This is the most actively contested variable in the field. Newer analyses argue that when you equate total volume, sets taken closer to failure produce modestly more growth. I have not independently verified that work, so I won't teach it as fact. What both camps agree on is the range: train roughly 1–3 RIR on most sets. When the evidence is a coin-flip and your mission is twenty more years of lifting, the tie goes to the option that doesn't bury you.

Failure is not the goal. Failure is a cost you pay only when it buys something — and here, it doesn't.

The rule: double progression

Two things about a set can go up — reps and load. The mistake is trying to move both at once, which leaves you unable to tell whether a bad session means the weight was heavy, the day was bad, or the programme is wrong. Double progression moves them one at a time.4

4. Feigenbaum, Barbell Medicine — Beyond Progressive Overload (R6). The worked pattern: "4–6 reps @ RPE 8 → hit 6, add load, reset to 4."

Write every main lift with a rep range and an RPE cap. For your split, the four main lifts become:

4 sets × 4–6 reps · stop each set at RPE 8 (2 reps in reserve)

Now climb the range on a fixed load. Once you fill it — top of the range, on every set, without exceeding the cap — you've earned the weight. Add load, drop back to the bottom of the range, and climb again.

Walk the rules in order. Stop at the first match.

The ordering is not decoration. Two conditions can be true of the same session, and without an order you would not know which to obey.5

5. The first version of this table listed five independent conditions. 6,6,6,6 @ RPE 10 matched two rows; 5,4,4,4 @ RPE 9 in week one matched none. Ordering fixes both.
#If…Then
1 Any set fell below 4 reps, or last set hit RPE 10 Drop load, rebuild up
2 Same load, no rep added, 3 sessions running Drop load, rebuild up
3 Every set hit 6 reps, RPE ≤ 8 Add load, reset reps
4 Last set hit RPE 9 Repeat load and reps
5 Otherwise — every set 4–6, RPE ≤ 8, not all at 6 Add reps, keep load

"Add load" means +2.5 kg upper body, +5 kg lower body — the smallest jump the plates allow. That increment is a practical convention, not something the research prescribes; the principle it serves is that load should follow demonstrated adaptation, never lead it.6

6. Feigenbaum calls this loading reactive, not proactive — Progressive Loading (R5).

When it stalls, reset — don't grind

Three sessions at the same load with no rep added is a stall. Drop to roughly 90% of that load and climb the range again. You will usually walk back through the old sticking point within a few weeks, because the reset lets fatigue clear while you keep training.

Honest labelling

Double progression and the 90% reset are coaching constructs. I looked for randomised trials on either and found none — the best available source is a practitioner article (R6). Treat them as reasoned practice with good mechanistic logic, not settled science. The RPE cap and the 1–3 RIR target, by contrast, do have peer-reviewed support.

Why your accessories only rest 60–90 seconds

The rule above governs the main lift. Rest time explains how the whole thing fits in 50 minutes. For strength, trained lifters need more than two minutes between sets to express it.7 For growth, resting past about 90 seconds showed no clearly detected additional benefit.8

7. Grgic et al., Sports Medicine 2018 — rest interval duration and strength (R10).
8. Singer et al., Front Sports Act Living 2024 — Bayesian meta-analysis on rest and hypertrophy (R11). Note the phrasing: no detected benefit is not the same as no benefit.

So the session splits cleanly: rest 2–3 minutes on the main lift, where the goal is strength, and 60–90 seconds on accessories, where the goal is size. That is why the accessories in your programme are supersetted. If reps or load fall off a cliff between supersetted sets, take longer — the clock serves the training, not the other way round.

Your knee changes three things

You told me you have a knee history with no current pain. The rule above is unchanged, but the movements it governs are not — see the five-day split for the swaps, and the progression protocol for the monitoring rule that tells you when to back off. Short version: pain ≤ 3/10, back to baseline within 24 hours, no swelling. Any of those fails, you regress the movement before you regress the weight.

The one thing to remember

Fill the rep range under the RPE cap, then add load and start over. Reps first, load second, never both. Stop at 2 reps in reserve. Three identical sessions means reset, not push.

Come back to this in two days

Don't reread this now — it will feel easy, and that feeling is a liar. Instead, on Thursday, open the progression protocol with the answers covered and try to reconstruct the five ordered rules from memory, in order. Recalling them cold builds retention in a way that rereading never does. Then log your next main-lift session and apply them for real.
