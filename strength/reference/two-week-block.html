The Two-Week Block

Ten sessions. Week one finds your loads. Week two makes them move. Exercise detail lives in the five-day split; the rule lives in the progression protocol. This page is only the schedule and the loading.

Why this block has no kilograms in it

You ran a leg session on Thursday 9 July, finished in about an hour, and the knee was clean — but the log never reached me: no weight, no per-set reps, no RPE. The ordered rule reads its input from exactly those numbers, so I will not invent a starting load and call it a plan.

Week one therefore establishes the loads instead of assuming them. That is not a workaround. It's what an intermediate should do entering any new block, and it quietly answers the biggest open question in this course: can you actually tell RPE 8 from RPE 9?

Same five sessions, twice. The exercises do not change between weeks — only the RPE cap and what you do with the bar. Changing exercises every week is how people avoid finding out whether they are progressing.

Week 1 · calibration — cap RPE 7

Every main lift: 4 sets × 4–6 reps, stop at RPE 7 (three reps in reserve). Yes, RPE 7, not 8. One notch lighter than the programme's real cap.

The job this week is not to progress. It is to find, for each of the four main lifts, the heaviest load at which a set of five still leaves three clean reps in the tank. Start deliberately too light and add weight between sets until you find it. If you finish a session thinking "I could have done more" — correct. That is the week working.

Finding the load, set by set

Ramp up as usual, then take your first working set at a weight you're confident is easy. Rate it honestly. If it came in under RPE 7, add 5 kg (lower body) or 2.5 kg (upper body) and take the next set there. Repeat. Whatever weight lands you at RPE 7 for a set of five is your week-two starting load. Write it down.

Four sets is enough to find it. Don't hunt past four.

The calibration test — Tuesday and Thursday only

Here is how you find out whether your RPE is honest. On Tuesday's lat pulldown and Thursday's seated cable row — machine movements, no spotter, nothing to drop — do this on the last set:

  1. Before you start, write down how many reps you predict you can do to true failure.
  2. Then do them. To actual failure — the point where a rep stalls despite full effort.
  3. Compare. Predicted 10 and got 10? Your internal gauge is calibrated.

Most intermediates undershoot by two to four reps. If you predicted 10 and hit 14, you were nowhere near failure when you thought you were — which means every "RPE 8" you have ever logged was really an RPE 6, and the whole progression rule has been running on a sensor that reads low. That is worth knowing before week two.

Do this twice, on those two machine exercises only. Never on the squat, never on the deadlift, never on the overhead press.

Week 2 · execution — cap RPE 8

Now the cap goes to RPE 8 (two reps in reserve), you start at the loads week one gave you, and after every main lift you walk the ordered rule — top to bottom, stop at the first line that fires.

#If…Then
1Any set below 4 reps, or last set RPE 10Drop load, rebuild up
2Same load, no rep added, 3 sessions runningDrop load, rebuild up
3Every set at 6 reps, RPE ≤ 8Add load, reset reps
4Last set at RPE 9Repeat load and reps
5Otherwise — all sets 4–6, RPE ≤ 8, not all 6Add reps, keep load

Across a two-week block you will most often land on rule 5. That is success. Adding a single rep to one set is progress; the plates come later. Rule 2 cannot fire inside this block — it needs three identical sessions, and you only get two.

If week one's calibration test embarrassed you

If you undershot your rep prediction by three or more, your RPE reads low. Do not compensate by adding weight. Instead, in week two, take the last set only of each main lift one rep further than feels right, and re-rate it. Your gauge recalibrates by being contradicted, not by being trusted harder.

What stays fixed all fortnight

  • The knee tripwire. Pain ≤ 3/10 and dull during the set; back to baseline by the next morning; no swelling, no warmth. Fail any one and regress — shorten the range of motion, then the load, then swap the movement. It held on 9 July; keep testing it.
  • Five minutes of easy cycling before lower days. Not before upper days.
  • Two-second descent on the first two working sets of squat and leg press.
  • Deadlift guardrails — see the split. If form degrades, or Monday's fatigue is still with you on Wednesday, cut to 2–3 sets or swap to trap-bar.
  • Rest: 2–3 min main lift, ~2 min secondary, 60–90 s supersetted accessories.

What happens on Sunday 26 July

Bring me the sheet. We do three things: read the rule across all four main lifts, check the calibration gap, and decide whether block three adds load, adds volume, or deloads. Two weeks is long enough to learn something and short enough that nothing has gone badly wrong yet — which is the whole point of running in blocks rather than drifting.

The one thing to remember

Week one is reconnaissance. Week two is the attack. Almost every lifter who stalls does so because they skipped week one and started at a weight their ego chose.
