Exercise Library

Cues, errors, benefits, harms — and, for each, how much of it is actually known. The session lessons link straight into these entries rather than restating them.

Read the evidence labels, or this page will mislead you

Almost no technique cue in lifting has been tested against injury outcomes in a randomised trial. What exists is biomechanics (torque models), EMG (which measures momentary muscle activation — not growth, not injury), and expert consensus. Each entry below is labelled Peer-reviewed — a study supports this specific claim — or Heuristic — coaching consensus and sound mechanical reasoning, with no study on the cue itself. The Smith machine squat needed two more: Convention (a number everyone repeats that nobody has measured) and Untested (a question the literature has simply never asked).

A heuristic is not worthless. It is just not a fact, and you deserve to know which is which.

Six things gyms teach that the evidence doesn't support

Three of these I told you myself before I checked. They're corrected here and in your programme.

The claimVerdictWhat's actually true
"Feet high on the leg press spares the knee" Not supported Escamilla (2001) compared high vs low directly: no significant difference in patellofemoral or tibiofemoral force at matched load. High placement does reduce how far the knee bends — use it for comfort, not as protection.1
"Knees must never pass the toes" Myth Restricting forward knee travel cut knee torque ~22% but raised hip torque roughly tenfold. The load doesn't vanish; it moves to your hips and lower back.2
"Deep squats destroy your knees" Myth Cruciate ligament loads are highest in partial ranges and fall as you descend. Heavy quarter-squats may be the worse long-term bet.3
"Slow eccentrics protect a healthy knee" Not supported Heavy slow eccentrics are proven for rehabilitating a diagnosed tendinopathy, not for preventing pain in an asymptomatic joint.4
"Belts weaken your core" Myth A belt raised intra-abdominal pressure and did not reduce trunk-muscle activity. It gives the core something to brace against.5
"Any pain means stop" Myth The validated pain-monitoring model permits loading pain up to 5/10, provided it doesn't climb week to week and returns to baseline by next morning.6
Your pain threshold, and why yours is stricter than the study's

Silbernagel's randomised trial — the origin of the pain-monitoring model — allowed pain up to 5/10. Your programme says 3/10. That gap is deliberate, and you should know it is my choice, not the study's finding.

Two reasons. The trial ran on Achilles tendons — load-tolerant tissue — not knees, so applying its numbers to your joint is already an extrapolation. And your mission is twenty more years of lifting, not a fast return to running. When you extrapolate, extrapolate conservatively. Stay at 3.

Lower body

Main lift · Tuesday

Back squat

The most bar weight your knees will see all week. It used to sit on Monday because technique degrades with fatigue; it now sits on Tuesday, one day back from the freshest slot, to keep two clear days between it and the deadlift. Your legs are still untouched when you get to it — the freshness it gives up is systemic, not local.

Cues

  1. Brace before you descend — breathe into the belly, stack the ribcage over the pelvis.
  2. Hips back and knees forward. Knees track over the toes; they do not cave inward.
  3. Lead out of the hole with the chest. Hips and shoulders rise together.
  4. Tripod foot: big toe, little toe, heel. Nothing lifts.

Common errors

  • Hips rising faster than the shoulders — the squat becomes a good morning.
  • Knee valgus (knees collapsing inward). The one error worth stopping a set for.
  • Heels or toes lifting off the floor.

Buys you

  • Quads, glutes, adductors, spinal erectors — and the bulk of your lower-body strength.

Costs you

  • The highest systemic fatigue of any lift except the deadlift. Knee valgus under load is the genuine injury mechanism here, not depth.
Your knee. Squat to the deepest position that stays painless — and understand that depth itself is not the villain. Cruciate loads are highest in partial ranges, so a controlled full squat is not more dangerous than a quarter squat.3 Forcing your knees back behind your toes merely dumps the load on your hips and lumbar spine.2 What actually matters: don't let the knee cave.

Heuristic for the cues (expert consensus — Barbell Medicine). Peer-reviewed for the depth and knee-travel claims.23

Squatting this on a Smith machine instead? That is a different lift with a different load column — but it is not a worse one. Read that entry before you next write a number down.

Main-lift substitute · Tuesday

Smith machine back squat

The same movement with the bar running on fixed vertical rails. The machine holds the bar's path for you, so the balancing and steering the free bar demands is done by the steel instead of by you. You squatted this in place of the prescribed back squat — so here is everything that changes, and the one large thing that doesn't.

First, the reassuring part — and it is the well-evidenced part

This is not a downgrade in adaptation. An 8-week randomised trial (n=46) trained one group on free weights and one on machines — the machine group squatted on a Smith. Squat strength rose 11–19% in both groups, with no significant difference between them, and no difference in quadriceps thickness.12 A 2023 meta-analysis comparing free weights against machines directly found no difference in dynamic strength, isometric strength, jump, or hypertrophy.13

If you trained hard on the Smith, you trained. Nothing was wasted. The problems below are all about bookkeeping and what we don't know — not about whether the session counted.

Cues

  1. Painless depth, exactly as on the free bar. Nothing about the rails changes the depth argument — cruciate loads are still highest in partial ranges.3
  2. Knees still do not cave. The machine constrains the bar. It does not constrain your knees. Valgus remains the one error worth stopping a set for.
  3. Set your feet slightly forward of the bar, because the fixed path makes a stacked, directly-under stance uncomfortable — then leave them there and record where they were. Foot position is a real variable on this machine (see the knee note); moving it week to week turns your log into noise.
  4. Write down which machine. Brand, or "the one by the far wall" — plus plates-per-side. Without the machine's identity the number in your log means nothing (see below).

Common errors

  • Logging the Smith load in the same column as your free-bar squat. This is the error that actually costs you something.
  • Leaning into the rails and letting the machine hold you up, rather than bracing.
  • Drifting foot position between sessions, then wondering why the lift feels different.
  • Assuming a heavier number means you got stronger, when you have only changed apparatus.

Buys you

  • Quads and glutes, loaded hard and progressively, with no meaningful loss of strength or size adaptation.1213 Also: you can push closer to a hard set alone, because there is nothing to dump.

Costs you

  • The stabilising and bar-steering skill itself. On a fixed path you cannot self-correct bar drift — because there is no drift to correct. That skill is real, the free bar trains it, and this does not. It is the honest loss here.
  • A load number that cannot be compared to anything outside this machine.
The number does not transfer. Give it its own column.

The adaptation carries over; the score does not. The 2023 meta-analysis found that gains are specific to the modality you test in — free-weight training favoured free-weight strength tests (SMD −0.210, p=0.023).13 And the folk belief that you always lift less on a Smith is simply wrong: Cotterman (2005) found squat 1RM was greater on the Smith than with free weights, reaching significance for women.14

So the error runs in both directions, and no correction factor exists. Never convert a Smith load into a free-bar load, or the reverse. Log it as its own lift, on its own line. Progression is unaffected — same machine, same bar, week over week, the delta is what the ordered rule reads, and the delta is honest.

Your knee — and here the honest answer is that nobody knows. No study has ever compared knee loading between a Smith squat and a free barbell squat in the same lifters. There are no trials of Smith squatting in people with patellofemoral pain or a knee history, and no pain or injury outcome data at all. If anyone tells you the Smith is easier on your knees, they are guessing.

What does exist is modelling within the machine: moving your feet forward lowers the modelled knee torque and compressive force1516 — but it does so by raising hip and lumbosacral torque. That is the identical bargain you already met at the back squat: restricting knee travel cut knee torque ~22% and raised hip torque roughly tenfold.2 Load is transferred, not removed. And Biscarini's model was run with no human subjects at all.

The rule that still governs is the one that always did: the deepest painless depth, no valgus, and the 3/10 knee tripwire. Those work on any apparatus.

The plates are not the load

Smith machines are not all the same machine. A peer-reviewed analysis identified three distinct resistance mechanisms in commercial units — a plainly constrained barbell, a counterweighted bar, and a viscous-resistance bar.17 Ignoring the counterweight made calculated force and power come out significantly lower at every load tested.18

Manufacturers state effective bar weights across roughly 3–20 kg — Hammer Strength ~13.6 kg, Matrix ~11.3 kg, Life Fitness Signature ~9 kg, Precor ~7–11 kg. Treat those as Convention, not measurement: no peer-reviewed survey has actually measured starting resistance across commercial machines.

Practical consequence: with 10 kg a side on a machine you have not identified, your true load sits somewhere in a band tens of kilograms wide. That is fine for progression — the band doesn't move, so the week-to-week delta is real. It is fatal for any comparison to a free bar.

The "43% more muscle activity" figure — the least useful number here

You will meet the claim that the free squat produces 43% more EMG than the Smith. It comes from a study of n=6, measuring a single acute set.19 Acute EMG is momentary activation — it is not growth, not strength, not adaptation.

Here is the part nobody quotes: the same research group then ran the actual training study — eight weeks, 46 people — and found no difference in outcomes.12 When the acute proxy and the training result disagree, the training result wins. Ignore the 43%.

Peer-reviewed that the Smith is not a downgrade in strength or hypertrophy adaptation,1213 and that the load number is modality-specific.1314 Convention for the 3–20 kg effective-bar-weight range — manufacturer claims, never measured.17 Untested for every claim about the Smith and your knee: modelling only, no free-bar comparison, no injury outcomes.1516

Secondary compound · Thursday

Front squat

Quad work with an upright torso, so your lower back gets a break on the day you deadlift.

Cues

  1. The bar sits on your shoulders. Your hands guide it; they do not hold it.
  2. Elbows up and driven forward, the whole set.
  3. Stay upright. Chest up.
  4. Brace hard against the rack position.

Common errors

  • Elbows drop → chest tips → the bar rolls off.
  • Upper back rounds under the rack.
  • Hips shoot back, turning it into a low-bar squat you cannot hold.

Buys you

  • Quads, upper back, and a brutally honest signal the moment your bracing fails.

Costs you

  • Wrist and elbow mobility demands. Much lighter loads than the back squat.
Your knee. An upright torso shifts work toward the quads and the knee — that is the point of the lift, not a defect. Your programme picked it over the hack squat, but be clear: that swap is my judgement, not a finding. No study compares them for knee health. If the front squat bothers the knee and the hack squat doesn't, believe your knee.

Heuristic — expert consensus (Juggernaut). The front-squat-over-hack-squat choice is unsupported by direct evidence.

Main lift · Thursday

Conventional deadlift

The heaviest thing you will pick up. Placed two days after squats so your lower back has cleared.

Cues

  1. Bar over midfoot — roughly over your shoelaces — before you touch it.
  2. Set the lats and "pull the slack out" of the bar before it moves.
  3. Brace, then hinge down to the bar. Your hip height is your own; there is no universal position.
  4. Push the floor away. Finish tall — don't lean back at the top.

Common errors

  • Bar drifting forward, away from the shins.
  • Hips shooting up first, leaving the bar behind.
  • Jerking the bar off the floor instead of pulling the slack out.

Buys you

  • Posterior chain, grip, spinal erectors — more total systemic strength per rep than anything else in the gym.

Costs you

  • The most fatigue of any lift in the programme. It is why the guardrails exist.
On "never round your back"

The honest version: a systematic review found only low-quality evidence, and it did not support lumbar flexion during lifting as a cause of back pain.7 The blanket prohibition is not proven. Brace to a near-neutral spine anyway — it is the most efficient way to move heavy loads and it buys you margin. Treat it as a skill, not a law.

Heuristic for cues (Stronger By Science). Peer-reviewed, and contested, on lumbar flexion.7

Secondary compound · Tuesday

Romanian deadlift

Hamstrings and glutes under a long, loaded stretch — with almost nothing asked of the knee.

Cues

  1. Hips travel straight back. The knee bends slightly, once, and holds that angle.
  2. Drag the bar down your thighs. It stays in contact.
  3. Stop at the end of the hamstring stretch — not at a fixed depth, and never past the point where your back rounds.
  4. Neutral spine, lats engaged, bar close.

Common errors

  • Bending the knees progressively — it becomes a stiff-legged squat.
  • Chasing depth past the point where the lumbar spine rounds.
  • Letting the bar drift away from the legs.

Buys you

  • Hamstrings at long muscle length, glutes, erectors, and a hinge pattern that transfers straight to the deadlift.

Costs you

  • Lower-back fatigue that carries into Thursday if you push it.
Your knee. One of the best exercises available to you: near-zero knee flexion under load, large hamstring and glute return. If a lower day has to lose an exercise, this is not the one.

Heuristic — coaching consensus; the loaded-stretch rationale aligns with long-muscle-length hypertrophy research (RP).

Accessory · Tuesday

Leg press

Quad volume without asking anything of your spine or your balance.

Cues

  1. Set depth by your pelvis, not the machine. Stop the moment your hips or lower back begin peeling off the pad.
  2. Knees track over the toes with a slight toe-out. They do not cave.
  3. Reach full extension under control. Don't snap into the stop, and don't force the knee past neutral.
  4. Whole foot stays on the platform.

Common errors

  • Going so deep the pelvis tucks and the lumbar spine rounds under load.
  • Bouncing into lockout.
  • Enormous weight through four inches of range.
I told you feet-high protects the knee. It doesn't.

Escamilla (2001) put ten experienced lifters through high and low foot placement with 3D motion capture, EMG, and a joint model. Patellofemoral compression, tibiofemoral compression, and PCL force were not significantly different between them.1

What high placement does do is reduce how far the knee bends. That is a real reason to use it if deep knee flexion is what irritates you — it is simply not the load-shedding I claimed. Keep your feet high if it feels better. Don't believe it is body armour.

Peer-reviewed on foot placement.1 Heuristic on the lockout caution — no study on leg-press lockout injury exists at all.

Accessory · Thursday

Bulgarian split squat

Unilateral leg work you can steer toward the hip and away from the knee — and unlike most such claims, this one has numbers behind it.

Cues

  1. Plant the front foot far forward — far enough that the shin is close to vertical at the bottom. This is the single adjustment that matters for you.
  2. Allow a slight forward torso lean. Let the hip take the work.
  3. Keep the front glute engaged so the knee does not cave inward.
  4. Control the descent; drive through the whole front foot, not the back one.

Common errors

  • Stride too short plus an upright torso → the knee travels far forward and eats the load.
  • Front knee caving in.
  • Pushing off the rear foot, which turns it into a bad lunge.
Your knee — and here the evidence agrees with me. Increasing step length from 50% to 120% of leg length raised the peak hip-extension moment by 15.7% and dropped the peak knee-extension moment by 15.1%, with more glute and hamstring involvement.8 A longer stride measurably moves load off the knee. It reduces knee load; it does not abolish it. If it still bites, swap to a B-stance Romanian deadlift — nearly all the glute, almost no knee flexion.

Peer-reviewed for the stride-length load shift. Acute biomechanics: the load shift is measured, the clinical benefit inferred.8

Accessory · Tuesday (seated) & Thursday (lying)

Leg curl

Direct hamstring work with no spinal load and no knee compression. Two variants, two sessions, so the hamstrings get trained at different hip angles.

Cues

  1. Full range, especially the stretched end — start from straight knees.
  2. Control the eccentric. Do not bounce out of the bottom; that is where hamstrings tear.
  3. Strap in tight so your hips cannot lift.
  4. Ignore toe angle. "Point your toes in for the inner hamstring" is folklore.

Common errors

  • Bouncing at the stretched position.
  • Half reps at the top.
  • Hips lifting off the pad to cheat the last few reps.
Your knee. Open-chain, minimal joint compression. One of the safest things in the building for you, and it directly counterbalances all the quad work.

Heuristic — coaching consensus (RP), where the toe-angle myth is explicitly rejected.

Accessory · Tuesday

Standing calf raise

The one muscle your programme knowingly under-trains — three sets a week is maintenance, and you were told so.

Cues

  1. Full range: heel below the platform at the bottom, all the way up at the top.
  2. Pause two seconds in the stretch to kill the Achilles bounce.
  3. Control the descent.
  4. Knees roughly straight, to bias the gastrocnemius.

Common errors

  • Bouncing on the Achilles through a short range.
  • Half reps.
  • Moving so fast nothing is loaded.

Heuristic — consensus. The full-range, stretched-position emphasis is consistent with long-muscle-length hypertrophy research, but no trial tests the pause cue itself.

Push

Main lift · Monday

Barbell bench press

Your main horizontal press, and the one upper-body lift where a spotter or safety pins are not optional.

Cues

  1. Consistent grip, consistent touch point, repeatable bar path. Repeatability is the technique.
  2. Shoulder blades back and down; a moderate arch.
  3. Feet planted, legs driving into the floor.
  4. Bar starts over the shoulder, touches the lower chest, presses back over the shoulder — a slight diagonal, not straight up.

Common errors

  • Chasing a textbook style that fights your own anatomy. Barbell Medicine's central warning.
  • Elbows flaring to 90° while the bar drifts toward the face.
  • Bouncing the bar off the ribcage.

Buys you

  • Chest, front delts, triceps — and the clearest strength number in the gym.

Costs you

  • Shoulder wear, if the bar path and elbow angle fight each other week after week.

Heuristic — expert consensus (Barbell Medicine).

Accessory · Monday (incline) & Wednesday (flat)

Incline & flat dumbbell press

Chest volume where each shoulder is free to find its own path — which a barbell does not allow.

Cues

  1. Incline bench at about 30°. Steeper turns it into a shoulder press.
  2. Retract and depress the shoulder blades before the first rep, and hold it there.
  3. Full range, each arm on the path that suits that shoulder.
  4. Touch around the upper chest. Don't clang the bells together at the top.

Common errors

  • Bench at 45° or more — now it is a delt exercise you are calling a chest exercise.
  • Losing the scapular set halfway through the set.
  • Over-lowering into a stretch the shoulder objects to.

Peer-reviewed (weakly) on the 30° angle — from EMG studies, which measure activation, not growth. Heuristic for everything else.

Main lift · Wednesday

Standing overhead press

The lift that exposes whether your bracing is real, because there is no bench to lie to you.

Cues

  1. Grip just outside shoulder width, heel of the hand under the bar.
  2. Elbows slightly in front at the start — pointing toward each other, not flared out.
  3. Get your head "through the window" so the bar finishes over your midfoot.
  4. Brace the glutes and abs. Do not hyperextend the lower back to fake a vertical bar path.

Common errors

  • Leaning back and pressing off an arched lumbar spine.
  • Elbows flaring wide.
  • Bar drifting forward at lockout, finishing in front of the shoulders.

Costs you

  • Lower-back extension load if you lean back. This, not the shoulder, is where the overhead press hurts people.

Heuristic — expert consensus (Untamed Strength).

Accessory · Friday

Close-grip bench press

The heaviest triceps work you will do, and half a chest exercise for free.

Cues

  1. Grip about shoulder width — roughly a fist narrower than your bench grip. Hands touching is not "closer grip"; it is a wrist injury with no extra triceps.
  2. Tuck the elbows to about 45°.
  3. Bar touches lower on the torso, following the tucked elbows.
  4. Wrists stacked over the elbows, neutral.

Common errors

  • Grip far too narrow — wrists and elbows pay, triceps gain nothing.
  • Flaring elbows, which hands the set back to the chest.
  • Forcing a high touch point.

Heuristic — expert consensus. The "no extra triceps from an ultra-narrow grip" claim is widely taught and, as far as I can find, untested.

Pull

Secondary compound · Wednesday

Weighted pull-up

Cues

  1. Earn it at bodyweight first. Added load amplifies every flaw you have.
  2. Initiate from the shoulder blades — depress and retract before the elbows bend.
  3. Full range: dead hang to chin clearly over the bar.
  4. Control the descent. A tight core kills the swing.

Common errors

  • Adding weight too early, which buys you kipping and half reps.
  • Skipping the scapular set, making it a biceps exercise.
  • Dropping out of the eccentric.

Heuristic — coaching consensus, no trial.

Accessory · Monday · calibration test, week 1

Lat pulldown

Vertical pulling volume — and, in week one, the safe place to find out whether your RPE is honest.

Cues

  1. Reach a full overhead position every rep, arms near-straight, slight stretch.
  2. Lead the descent by pulling the elbows down and in, about 10–15° from the torso.
  3. Chest up, torso angle fixed for the whole set.
  4. Grip width barely matters. Pick one and progress it.

Common errors

  • Leaning back further each rep to keep the set alive.
  • Cutting the top range short.
  • Chasing "lat feel" instead of load, effort, and progression.
Week 1: this is where you go to failure

Predict your reps-to-failure, write it down, then take the last set to actual failure and compare. A machine, no spotter, nothing to drop. Never run this test on the squat, the deadlift, or the overhead press.

Heuristic — expert consensus (Barbell Medicine).

Secondary compound · Monday

Chest-supported row

Back thickness without asking your erectors to hold you up — which is exactly why it, and not a barbell row, follows a week containing Romanian deadlifts.

Cues

  1. Drive the elbows back and up. Do not curl the handle toward you.
  2. Let the shoulder blades protract at the bottom and retract at the top.
  3. Chest stays glued to the pad the entire set.
  4. A one-to-two second pause at the top, if you like.

Common errors

  • Peeling off the pad to generate momentum.
  • Range shortening as the set gets hard.
  • Stopping several reps short of a hard set.

Heuristic — expert consensus (Barbell Medicine).

Accessory · Wednesday · calibration test, week 1

Seated cable row

Cues

  1. Drive the elbows back. You are not "pulling the handle to your body."
  2. Stay near vertical — 10–15° of lean, not a rowing-machine heave.
  3. Repeatable path, repeatable end position.
  4. Let the shoulder blades protract at the stretch, retract at the finish.

Common errors

  • Progressively more torso English as fatigue arrives.
  • Range shortening.
  • Stopping short of a genuinely hard set.

Heuristic — expert consensus (Barbell Medicine).

Accessory · Monday

Face pull

Rear delts and external rotators — the counterweight to a programme with a lot of pressing in it.

Cues

  1. Elbows lead, high and wide, toward your eyes. Finish with external rotation.
  2. Keep the traps quiet. No shrug.
  3. Light load, controlled, higher reps. This is not a strength lift.
  4. Cable set at roughly face height.

Common errors

  • Shrugging, so the traps do the work.
  • Loading it heavily enough that it becomes a high row.
  • No external rotation at the finish, which removes the point of the exercise.

Heuristic for the cues. No EMG study exists on the "face pull" as such — though the two actions it blends (external rotation and prone horizontal abduction) are strongly supported for infraspinatus and posterior deltoid recruitment.9

Accessory · Monday (cable) & Wednesday (dumbbell)

Lateral raise

Your side delts sit at about six sets a week — maintenance, not growth. If they matter to you, we move sets from elsewhere. We do not add time.

Cues

  1. Lead with the elbow. Raise to about shoulder height, no higher.
  2. Keep tension constant — a cable, or a slight forward lean, loads the bottom where dumbbells go slack.
  3. Minimal swing. Control the way down.
  4. Don't shrug.

Common errors

  • Swinging the weight up with traps and hips.
  • Too heavy, so it becomes a shrug-row.
  • Dropping the eccentric entirely.

Peer-reviewed (weakly): the lateral raise produced the highest medial-deltoid activation among the exercises tested.10 That is acute EMG — activation, not growth. The cues are heuristic.

Accessory · Wednesday

Rear delt fly

Cues

  1. Horizontal abduction with a slight elbow bend. Lead with elbows and pinkies; stop at about shoulder height.
  2. A neutral or thumbs-down grip may bias the posterior delt slightly.
  3. Don't hard-retract the shoulder blades first — that turns it into a rhomboid exercise.
  4. Light, controlled, chest-supported if you have the option.

Common errors

  • Torso swing.
  • Converting it into a scapular-retraction row.
  • Too heavy → high row.

Heuristic, with weak EMG support for the grip effect. Treat the grip detail as marginal.

Arms

Accessory · Friday

Curls — barbell, incline dumbbell, hammer

Three curls, three shoulder and elbow positions. The incline variant trains the biceps at long muscle length; the hammer variant hits brachialis and brachioradialis.

Cues

  1. The upper arm stays fixed at your side. Elbow drift hands the work to your front delt.
  2. No torso swing, no hip drive.
  3. Control the eccentric. Full stretch at the bottom — especially on the incline curl, which exists for exactly that.
  4. Neutral wrist. Your wrists and elbows have to last twenty years too.

Common errors

  • Elbows drifting forward as the set gets hard.
  • Swinging the body.
  • Cutting the bottom of the range — the half that matters.

Heuristic — expert consensus and mechanical reasoning. The popular "elbows forward means less biceps" demonstration rests on one coach's EMG clip, not a trial.

Accessory · Friday

Triceps — pushdown & overhead extension

Both, not either. This is the one place your programme's exercise pairing is doing real work.

Cues

  1. Upper arms pinned to your sides. Only the forearm moves.
  2. Full lockout, brief squeeze, controlled eccentric.
  3. Pair the pushdown with an overhead extension. The pushdown never lengthens the long head of the triceps; the overhead extension does.
  4. Spreading the rope at the bottom is, at best, marginal.

Common errors

  • Elbows travelling forward — now it is a press.
  • Leaning over the stack and pushing with bodyweight.
  • Doing only pushdowns, then wondering why the long head never grows.

Peer-reviewed, and the reason both movements sit in your Friday: training the triceps at long muscle length (overhead) beats short-length work (pushdown) for growth.11

Core

Accessory · Thursday

Hanging leg raise

Cues

  1. Tilt the pelvis backwards before the legs rise. Curl the tailbone toward the ribs. This is the whole exercise; without it you are training hip flexors.
  2. Think "ribs toward pelvis," not "lift the legs."
  3. No swinging.
  4. Arms relatively straight.

Common errors

  • Hip-flexor dominance: thighs swing up, pelvis never tilts.
  • Pendulum swinging between reps.
  • Lumbar hyperextension. If you cannot hold the tilt, regress to a bent-knee raise. Your back will thank you in a decade.

Heuristic — rests on functional anatomy. No study isolates the pelvic-tilt cue.

Accessory · Friday

Cable crunch

Cues

  1. Flex the spine, not the hips. Curl the sternum toward the pubic bone.
  2. Lock the hips. Thighs stay vertical and still.
  3. Hands fixed relative to your head — let the abs pull you down, don't drag the rope with your arms.
  4. Actively round the back. This is the one lift where a rigid neutral spine is wrong — it is the opposite of the deadlift.

Common errors

  • Hinging at the hips instead of flexing the spine. The number-one error.
  • Pulling with the arms and lats.
  • Holding a rigid spine, then wondering why nothing happens.

Heuristic — consensus grounded in the fact that the rectus abdominis is a trunk flexor. No trial verifies the cues.
