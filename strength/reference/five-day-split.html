The Five-Day Split

Upper/lower, Monday to Friday, 45–60 minutes. Every main lift carries a rep range and an RPE cap, so the progression protocol applies to it directly.

Tinted rows are the main lift — the one you rate, log, and progress. Exercises sharing a letter (A1/A2) are supersetted. Knee marks a modification made for your knee history.

How to read this

Main lift: 4 × 4–6 reps, stop at RPE 8, rest 2–3 min. Rate the last set and walk the ordered rule.
Accessories: stay 1–3 RIR, rest 60–90 s, superset the paired ones. Progress them by adding reps first, then load — same rule, looser cap.

Your knee, before anything else

You have a knee history and no current pain. The programme below is modified, not restricted. Three standing rules, every session that loads the knee:

1. Pain during a set stays ≤ 3/10, dull, never sharp. 2. Next morning you're back to your normal baseline. 3. No swelling, no warmth.

Fail any one and regress in this order: shorten the range of motion, then drop the load, then swap the movement. Two bad sessions on the same lift means stop guessing and see a physiotherapist. I'm not a clinician; this isn't medical advice.

Squat: go to the deepest position that stays painless — but know that depth itself is not the danger; cruciate loads are highest in partial ranges. What matters is that the knee never caves inward. Leg press: set depth by your pelvis, not the machine — stop before your hips peel off the pad. Feet higher on the platform is fine for comfort: it reduces how far the knee bends, but a direct comparison found no significant difference in knee-joint forces (see the library). Don't slam into lockout. RDL: stop when your lower back rounds, not when your hamstrings scream.

Two days after the squat, and five days before the next one. That spacing is the whole reason the week is ordered this way — don't shuffle the days without thinking it through.

Front squat, not hack squat. The original programme offered either; the hack squat pins your torso and drives the knees forward under load, which is the wrong trade for you. Bulgarian split squat: take a longer stride and let the torso lean slightly forward. That converts it from a knee-dominant lunge into a hip-dominant one. If it still bites, swap it for a B-stance Romanian deadlift, which keeps the unilateral work and nearly all the glute stimulus with almost no knee flexion. Deadlift: reset every rep. No touch-and-go at this intensity.

Deadlift guardrails — back off when any of these is true

Weekly heavy pulling is fine for an intermediate with tripwires. Cut to 2–3 sets, swap to trap-bar or higher-rep RDL, or deload 10–15% if:

· form degrades on the last set (back rounds, bar drifts forward), or
· low-back fatigue from Tuesday's squat and RDL is still with you on Thursday, or
· the last set exceeds RPE 8 for two straight weeks, or
· any pain at all, anywhere.

Warming up

I have now been wrong about this twice. Here is the honest version.

First I told you to skip the cardio warm-up. Then, on learning about your knee, I reversed and called five minutes on a bike "not optional." Both were overstated.

What the evidence actually shows: structured neuromuscular warm-ups reduce lower-limb injury in trials, but that signal comes from targeted drills, not generic cycling. The Cochrane review on knee exercise evaluates whole programmes and never isolates the warm-up. There is no good evidence that five minutes on a bike prevents a lifting injury.

Do it anyway. It costs five minutes, raises tissue temperature, and makes the first ramp set feel better. Just understand that the ramping squat sets are the part doing the work — they warm the exact joint at the exact angles you are about to load.

Lower days: five easy minutes on a bike or rower first — no resistance worth mentioning, just movement. Then three or four ramping sets on the main lift: empty bar, then roughly 50%, 70%, and 85% of your working weight, for progressively fewer reps.

Upper days: skip the bike, ramp straight into the main lift.

Control the descent on squats and leg presses. I previously called a two-second eccentric "the cheapest knee insurance available" — that was wrong. Heavy slow eccentrics are well evidenced for rehabilitating a diagnosed tendinopathy, not for protecting a knee that doesn't hurt. Control the weight because control is how you stop the knee caving and the pelvis tucking, not because the tempo is prophylactic.

Read the trade honestly

Working ranges of 10–20 hard sets per muscle per week come from Barbell Medicine (R7) and the volume literature (R8), with returns falling off sharply past ~20 (R3). Calves, side delts and rear delts sit below that here. That is a choice the 45–60 minute constraint forced, not an oversight. If any of them matter to you, say so — we move sets, we don't add time.

Two caveats on the numbers themselves. They are my arithmetic on your programme, not a cited finding; the fractional set convention behind them is Nuckols' (R1). And the main lifts are counted at 4–6 reps, just below the rep band in which hard-set counting was validated (R12) — so treat the whole table as a good approximation, not a measurement.

If you're eating at maintenance

Expect strength to progress slowly, and judge the programme on whether you are holding your lifts, not adding to them. The ordered rule still applies — you will simply spend longer on rule 5 ("add reps, keep load") and reach rule 3 less often. That is not a stall.
