# I programmed before I screened — and the learner caught it

Recorded 2026-07-09.

I designed a five-day split, taught a progression rule, and published three pages **before ever
asking whether anything in his body was injured.** The learner asked directly: "why you never ask
for my physical conditions before giving instructions?" He was right, and the omission was not
cosmetic — I had prescribed weekly conventional deadlifts at 4×4–6 @ RPE 8, Bulgarian split squats,
and weighted pull-ups to someone I knew nothing about physically.

The sharpest part: [[MISSION.md]] says the goal is **longevity**. Screening is the most
mission-relevant question available, and I skipped straight to programming. I optimised for the
stated goal while ignoring the body that has to execute it.

## What screening revealed
- **Knee history, currently pain-free.** No medical restrictions. Age 30–45.
- Changed three concrete things: leg press foot position (higher), Bulgarian split squat (longer
  stride, hip-dominant; B-stance RDL as the escape hatch), and hack squat removed in favour of front
  squat.
- **Reversed my own warm-up advice.** I had written "skip the cardio warm-up." For a knee with a
  history that is wrong; lower days now open with five easy minutes of cyclic work.
- Added a monitoring rule (pain ≤3/10, baseline within 24 h, no swelling) and a not-a-clinician
  disclaimer to every page that prescribes load.

## Implications
- **Screen before programming, always.** Injury history, medical flags, current pain, and age belong
  in the very first interview alongside goal and equipment — not after three pages have shipped.
- The learner audits what I give him and pushes back when the reasoning has a hole. That is a strong
  signal: teach the *reasoning*, expose uncertainty, and expect it to be tested. It is also why the
  evidence-status labelling in `reference/progression-protocol.html` matters more than usual here.
- Future lessons touching lower-body loading must route through the knee modifications rather than
  restating the generic programme.
