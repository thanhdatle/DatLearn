The Transferable System

How to teach Kai anything memorable. The phone number is only the first thing you will run through this loop.

This is the mission's second deliverable. The number expires. The loop doesn't.

The whole thing in one sentence

Turn it into a song with three chunks; sing it at him until he owns it; then quiz only by cueing and answering instantly; once a day, not five times; everywhere, not just on the sofa.

Every step below is one of those clauses, with its reason attached. The reasons matter. When you hit new material and the loop seems to be failing, the fix is always "which step did I skip?" — and you can only ask that if you know what each step was for.

1 · The five-step loop

  1. ENCODE — as a song, a rhythm, or a story. Never as a list.

    Give the material structure that carries itself. A familiar melody is best; a strong rhythm second; a story third. Three chunks maximum — a preschooler's pure chunk capacity is about 2–2.5 (R2), so three is already at the ceiling and five is double it.

    Use a tune he already knows — a new melody is a second thing to learn; a known melody is free. And never vary it once chosen: when the tune changed between verses, sung material was recalled worse than spoken material (R5). A different tune each time is worse than no tune at all.
  2. OVERLEARN — before you test. This is the step everybody skips.

    Present it, sing it, repeat it — for days — before you ask a single question. In preschoolers, when initial learning was low, retrieval practice produced a benefit of d = 0.034, p = .738: nothing at all (R10). The authors' own words: "ensure that new information is presented multiple times before transitioning to retrieval-based practices."

    Testing early doesn't buy a head start. It buys zero — and it teaches him he is bad at this.
  3. CUED RECALL + IMMEDIATE FEEDBACK — the only quiz that works.

    Give him the start, let him supply the rest, tell him instantly whether he was right. Four experiments on preschoolers (R9): free recall → no effect at all; cued recall → ~17%; cued recall plus immediate feedback → ~47%.

    Never ask an open question ("what's the…?"). And never let an error stand — give the answer, then immediately hand him a rep he can win. Practising an error installs the error (R21).
  4. SPACE IT — daily, then weekly, then monthly.

    One session a day beats four sessions today, and the gap is large: on the harder measure, spaced practice scored 3.08 vs 1.75, ηp² = 0.478 (R11). Two sessions in one day was indistinguishable from four.

    Then stretch the interval: daily → every other day → twice a week → weekly → monthly. And keep the success rate high. If he is failing, go back to presenting it — do not drill harder. Retrieval only helps when retrieval succeeds.
  5. VARY — the place, the asker, the mood.

    2.5-year-olds fell apart when the test context differed from the training context. 3-year-olds held up — but only if they had been trained across multiple contexts (R12). The ability to decontextualise comes online precisely when you train it.

    So: different rooms, different times, different moods (tired, silly, mildly frustrated — never genuinely frightened), and different people asking. An adult who isn't you is the most valuable variation available, because it is the closest rehearsal of the real thing. Knowledge learned only on the sofa risks being cued only by the sofa.
And the sixth thing — which isn't a step, it's a stance

Stop while it's still fun. Praise every chunk. Never drill a tired child. None of the five steps survive a child who has decided this is a chore. You are not running a curriculum. You are keeping a game alive long enough for it to do its work.

The most useful line in this entire workspace

A four-year-old will not rehearse on his own. An adult must supply the rehearsal. Every technique in the table above fails for the same underlying reason: they are all strategies a child runs on himself — and self-directed strategy is exactly the thing that is not built yet.

Until about seven, you are not teaching him to remember. You are being the part of him that remembers. That is the correct mental model for your job, and it is why every step of the loop is something you do.

4 · From singing it to saying it — the fading ladder

Expect this failure. It is the most predictable one there is: "He can SING it but he can't SAY it." The melody becomes a retrieval cue he cannot detach from — and a stressed adult may not think to let a child sing.

The fix rests on a specific finding: when presentation rate is equated, the sung advantage shrinks or disappears (R8). Much of a song's power is simply that it forces you to go slow, in a fixed, repeating structure. So you can drop the pitch and keep the rhythm. That is the whole trick.

  1. Week 1 — SING it.

    Full melody, full pitch. Exactly as taught.

  2. Week 2 — sing it, then CHANT it.

    Straight after singing, say it on a flat monotone — same rhythm, same pauses, same chunk boundaries. Pitch is the only thing you drop.

  3. Week 3 — chant it, then SPEAK it.

    Normal speaking voice. Keep the pauses. The chunk boundaries are the last thing to go — and in truth they never go.

  4. Week 4 — speak it COLD.

    Out of context, no run-up: "Hey — what's my number?" while making breakfast. This is the real target behaviour, and it is the only rung that actually tests it.

Honest labelling

This ladder is engineered, not cited. Its components are sourced — the presentation-rate finding (R8) and the chaining literature (R21) — but no source prescribes this exact four-rung ladder. It is a reasoned construction. It is the most speculative thing in this workspace, and you should know that while you are using it.

A useful intermediate rung if he gets stuck: clap the rhythm with no tune at all. It is an explicit halfway house between song and speech — and it teaches him how many digits there are, which is the thing that stops a child confidently stopping at six.

The drill that actually rehearses the target

Role-play an adult who asks. Play shop, or doctor, or hotel check-in — and make him "register" with his name and the number. Then have the shop call back and need it again. Then they need it for the delivery. (R26)

You cannot practise this by singing to yourself. The target behaviour is speaking a sequence to a person who is asking for it — and the only way to practise that is to have a person ask for it. Swap roles. Get a friend to play the asker. That is step 5 (VARY) and step 3 (CUED RECALL) doing their work at the same time.

Two rules when you start something new

One: never teach two of the same kind of thing at once. Two phone numbers in parallel is a documented way to lose both. Master one, then start the next.

Two: a new song needs a genuinely different tune. Same tune + different payload = interference, and you will corrupt the number you already have. The "never vary the melody" rule (R5) cuts both ways: one tune, one payload, forever.

When you want to teach Kai the next thing — his address, his full name, a safety rule — bring it to me and we'll encode it together. The hard part is never the loop; it's the encoding. Picking three good chunks and a tune that doesn't collide with one he already owns is genuinely worth ten minutes of conversation, and it is the difference between a week and a month.
