1 · The song

Tune: Frère Jacques — the same melody as Kìa Con Bướm Vàng (R27). Kai already knows the tune, so all his attention goes to the digits. Sing it slowly. A large part of a song's power is simply that it forces you to slow down and repeat a fixed structure.R8

Zero filler. Every note carries a digit or the call to action. And notice what the melody does for you for free: because line 5 is six syllables long, 286 — the hard tail — gets sung four times per pass, while 145 gets sung twice.

Use his word for you — then never change it

"Call my dad!" / "Call my mum!" = 3 syllables, an exact fit on "Ding dang dong." "Dad-dy" or "Bố ơi" = 4 syllables — split the last note into two quick beats. Either works. Picking one and sticking to it is the part that matters.

2 · The butterfly — the hook to teach out loud

It starts 086. It ends 286.
The eight-six flies away — and comes back.

Tell it as a story: the yellow butterfly of Kìa Con Bướm Vàng flies off, and returns at the end. This is the highest-leverage single fact in the number. Every practitioner source agrees the last four digits are the hardest and take the longest (R22) — and this hook converts the hardest chunk into a callback to the easiest one.

And drill the middle hardest

0862145286 holds 86 twice and 2 three times. Repeated material is what makes digits swap places. 145 is the only chunk with no repeats — it is the anchor that stops the two 86s collapsing into each other. If Kai ever sings "oh eight six two… two eight six," he has skipped the anchor. Go back to 145.

3 · Five minutes a day. Never more.

One short session a day beats four sessions today, and the difference is large (R11, ηp² = 0.478). Embed it in what you already do. Never make it a lesson. Never drill a tired or cranky child. Stop while it is still fun.

⚠️ The green call button — do not skip this

From a 2nd-grade teacher via the RAD Kids programme, reported by The Many Little Joys (R23): "so many kids fail at dialing 9-1-1 because they know the numbers, but they forget to press the call button."

Knowing the digits is not the same as being able to place the call. Teach the whole chain: unlock → phone app → dial → press green. The Cheeky Homemaker (R24) bakes it into the song's last line as "Green Means Go." Once the digits are solid, consider swapping lines 7–8 to "Press the green!" — 3 syllables, an exact fit.

4 · The backward-chaining ladder

He gets the first few digits and stalls. This is universal — and this is the fix. You say almost all of it; he lands the ending. Then you hand him one more digit each time, working backwards.R21

  1. You say almost all of it0 8 6 2 1 4 5 2 8 … → he says 6
  2. Hand him one more0 8 6 2 1 4 5 2 … → he says 8 6
  3. And another0 8 6 2 1 4 5 … → he says 2 8 6
  4. Now the hard chunk — and he owns it0 8 6 2 1 4 … → he says 5 2 8 6
  5. Over the anchor0 8 6 2 … → he says 1 4 5 2 8 6
  6. Silence — it's his(you say nothing) → 0 8 6 2 1 4 5 2 8 6
Why backward, and not forward

He ends on a success every single rep. He lands the final digit every time — and it attacks the weak tail first instead of last. Forward chaining does the opposite: it drills the part he already knows and abandons him at the part he doesn't. Pair it with the butterfly hook and the tail stops feeling like a cliff and starts feeling like a homecoming.

Recommended independently by a board-certified behaviour analyst (R21) and by a teacher who runs it with a written model, erasing digits right-to-left. Two sources, no contact between them, same technique.

6 · What to expect — and what is not failure

Read this before you get discouraged

Plan for 2–6 weeks of daily micro-practice — and know that this is a planning number assembled from parent anecdotes, not a research finding. No controlled study has ever taught ten digits to a three-year-old. The most credible practitioner voice available, an ex-first-grade teacher, expects a four-year-old not to manage the whole number, and says: "Don't be discouraged if they don't learn their phone numbers until they are even 6 or 7." (R22)

Melody buys durability, not speed (R7). Day one will not feel faster. Day thirty is the payoff. Do not read a slow first week as the method failing.

And while you wait — the tag

The AAP does not recommend relying on memorization. It recommends ID cards and wristbands (R17). The tag saves you on day 3. The song saves you on day 300. Deploy both — see the lost-child protocol.

If he stalls on the same chunk for more than a week, don't drill harder — tell me and we'll change the approach. A stall means retrieval is failing, and retrieval only helps when retrieval succeeds. The fix is almost always to go back to singing it to him, not to push.
