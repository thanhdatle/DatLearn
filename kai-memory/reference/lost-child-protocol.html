The Lost-Child Protocol

The number is step 5 of six — not a standalone skill. Build the routine; the digits plug into it.

Print this and stick it on the wall. Rehearse it like a fire drill.

The premise — say it plainly

Children under eight often cannot reliably recite a phone number under stress. A frightened, crying three-year-old is functionally in that category regardless of what he can sing at bath time — stress impairs memory retrieval in children, and the worst window falls more than 20 minutes after the fright, which is exactly where a lost child sits (R14).

So the number is not the plan. The routine is the plan — because an automatized routine lives in the habit system, and the habit system is what stress switches on (R13). And the tag in his jacket is the insurance — which is why §4 is not optional.

1 · The six steps

  1. FREEZE. Hug something.

    Stop moving. Give him a physical thing to hold — a pillar, a bench, the shop counter. Abstract "stay put" does not survive panic; an object to hold does. This is the oldest rule in child search-and-rescue: adults search in expanding circles, so a moving child outruns the circle forever (Hug-A-Tree, R20 — the programme itself notes the "tree" can be "a large rock or bench at a park, or a sales counter at a mall").
  2. SHOUT. Be loud.

    Yell for you — by the name he actually calls you, loudly, repeatedly. "Shout it big, strong, and loud, and we will come find you!" (KinderCare, R19). And you shout too — both of you shouting recruits every bystander in earshot.
  3. COUNT TO 50, out loud.

    Optional, and excellent. It gives a panicking child a structured task — which is the thing that actually prevents wandering. It takes about 40 seconds, and most reunions happen inside two minutes. Bonus: a loudly-counting child is an audio beacon.

    But note: Kai probably cannot count to 50 — "counts to 10" is a five-year milestone (R16). Use whatever he can count to, or — better — have him sing the phone song. The point is the structured task, not the number. And a child singing his father's phone number is the single most useful noise he could possibly be making.
  4. FIND A SAFE ADULT — a mum with children, first.

    "Don't talk to strangers" is actively harmful here — by definition, everyone around a lost child is a stranger. He needs a target, not a prohibition.

    Lead with "a mummy with kids." For the youngest children this beats the uniform rule, because "a store clerk can be hard for young children to identify" — a uniform is an abstraction; a lady with a baby is a picture (R19). Then, as he gets older, add the other two: someone in a uniform (police, security, staff with a badge), and someone behind a counter — they can't easily leave their station, which is what makes them a safe-enough ally.
  5. THE SCRIPT — one sentence, then the number.

    Name and number come after an adult is engaged and listening. The opening line stays short. See §2.
  6. NEVER GO ANYWHERE with anyone.

    He must insist on staying in the same building or area rather than going with someone somewhere else — even a helpful someone (Kidpower, R18). A helper can make a phone call from exactly where they are standing. There is never a reason to walk away — and this is the rule that matters most.

2 · The script

Sentence one, on its own. Short enough to survive fear:

"I'm lost. Please help me find my dad."

(Or simply: "I'm lost." A real four-year-old used exactly that — "I'm [name] and I'm lost" — on a park employee, and it worked. Keep the opener to one sentence and you have already won.)

Then, once the adult is listening — the number. He sings it, or he says it, in his chunks:

"My dad's number is oh-eight-six-two … one-four-five … two-eight-six."
Why not "086 214 5286"?

Because that is a different chunking — and Kai must only ever learn one: 0862 · 145 · 286, the same boundaries he sings. Handing him a second grouping for the highest-stakes retrieval of his life is exactly what makes digits swap places, and it breaks the one rule this whole course is built on. The digits are identical, and any adult who hears them can dial them.

The dialing rhythm — 086 214 5286 — belongs on the tag and on the keypad, both of which are read by an adult rather than produced by a child. Write it that way in §4. Never in his mouth.

Tell him this explicitly: "If you can't remember it — just SING it."

Singing is a valid output. A four-year-old once sang her phone number at a supermarket checkout and it worked perfectly well. If the spoken version deserts him under stress, the song is still there — it is stored in the system that stress leaves running. Give him explicit permission to use it, because a frightened child will not think to ask.

3 · Rehearse the unhelpful adult — almost nobody does this

Most parents rehearse a kind, attentive adult who crouches down and listens. But the freeze happens when the first adult is busy, distracted, or dismissive — and that is the far more likely encounter. Kidpower is the only source that drills it, and it may be the most valuable thing on this page.R18

You play…He must…
The cashier who says "wait in line." Persist: "But I'm lost!"
The adult on the phone, not looking down. Interrupt: "Excuse me, I need your help. I'm lost."
The adult who says "come with me, I'll help you look." Stay put. "Please call my dad. I have to stay here."
The adult who has the number but hasn't called yet. Hand it over: "This is my dad's number. Please call him!"

Run the "I'll help you look" one until it is boring. It carries the highest stakes and meets the least natural resistance — a three-year-old's instinct is to accept help from a confident adult, and that instinct is exactly the one we are overwriting.

4 · The backup ID — mandatory, not optional

This is what the AAP actually recommends

Read the AAP's page on a child going missing, and note what it does not say: it never tells parents to have children memorize a phone number. What it recommends is "name and address cards or identifying wristbands and medic alert tags" (R17).

The tag saves you on day 3. The song saves you on day 300. Deploy both, starting today. Memorization is not the safety net until it is reliable — and at three, it is not.

Note the chunking on the tag: 086 214 5286. That is the dialing rhythm, and it is the right one there — a tag is read by an adult who is about to punch it into a keypad. It is not the grouping Kai learns. See §2.

5 · Tone — this is a fire drill, not a warning about the world

Get this wrong and you buy a frightened child instead of a capable one

KinderCare's safety director: "don't tell her that the world is a big, scary place full of people who might harm her — after all, she may in fact need to rely on strangers for help." (R19)

Rehearsal done well reduces anxiety; it does not raise it. Children who have practised feel capable. Children who haven't feel vulnerable. The frame that lands is the one every child already understands: this is a fire drill. Nobody thinks a fire drill means the school is about to burn down.

Honest labelling: the "rehearsal reduces anxiety" claim is attributed to AAP guidance by a secondary source, and I could not confirm it on a first-party AAP page. Treat it as plausible but unverified. What is first-party AAP is the instruction to rehearse (R17) — and Kidpower and KinderCare both build entire programmes on it.

Also: mark base camp

On every big outing, agree a landmark before you set off — "if we get split up, I'll come back to the big red sign." A three-year-old cannot navigate to it, but he can often stay near it — and it gives step 1 somewhere to happen. It costs ten seconds.

Rehearse the unhelpful adult until it bores him. That single drill is the difference between a child who freezes and a child who persists — and it is the one nearly every parent skips.
