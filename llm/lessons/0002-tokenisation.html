The Model Never Sees Letters

Stage one of the pipeline you're here to build. You'll train a real tokeniser — the same algorithm GPT-2 uses — and watch it spell a word it has never seen.

Last lesson I let something slide. I said your model reads tokens, then quietly used words as tokens because it kept the counting legible. Real models don't do that, and the reason they don't is the whole of this lesson. Before a single parameter is learned, before any neural network exists, text has to become numbers. That conversion is a separate program with its own training run, and its choices are permanent.

Its own stage. The tokeniser is trained before the model, on its own corpus, with its own algorithm — no gradients, no neural network. In nanochat it is literally the first script you run.

Two obvious ideas, both wrong

You need to turn the cat sat into a list of integers. There are two things anyone would try first.

One token per character. Tiny vocabulary — a hundred or so symbols, and every word in every language is spellable. But now the cat sat is eleven tokens instead of three, and a page of text is thousands. Since the model's cost grows with sequence length, and its context is a fixed number of tokens, you have just made every document several times more expensive and cut your effective memory to a fraction.

One token per word. Short sequences, but the vocabulary is now every word anyone has ever written — millions, and still growing. Worse: the first time a user types a word you never saw, you have no token for it at all. That's the out-of-vocabulary problem, and it is fatal. There is no integer for lowest if lowest wasn't in your list.

Characters generalise but are long. Words are short but break. The answer is to let the data decide where the boundaries go.

Byte-pair encoding: the same move as last lesson

Here is the algorithm, and you already know how it works, because it is counting pairs — exactly what you did to build the bigram.1

1. Byte-pair encoding began as a text-compression trick and was adapted to language by Sennrich, Haddow & Birch, Neural Machine Translation of Rare Words with Subword Units (ACL 2016). It is what GPT-2, GPT-4, Llama, and GLM all use, with variations.

    What just happened

    Nine merges took the vocabulary from 11 symbols to 20, and the corpus from 95 tokens to 31 — a third of its original length. You bought shorter sequences with a bigger vocabulary. That is the only trade tokenisation ever makes.

    And then the important part. lowest never appears in that corpus. Yet the tokeniser encodes it without hesitating:

    Never seen"lowest"
    →
    A merge gavelow
    +
    A merge gaveest_
    →
    2 tokensboth known

    It learned low from low and lower. It learned est_ from newest and widest. It never saw them together — and it composed them anyway. Type slow and you'll see it fall back further, to s + low_, spelling out the part it doesn't know.

    That is why out-of-vocabulary words cannot happen here. In the worst case the tokeniser retreats all the way to single characters. It always has something. This single property is why BPE won, and why the word-level tokenisers of the 2010s are gone.

    Your tokeniser never saw lowest. What does it do with it?

    The dial, and where real models set it

    Stop merging early and you get a small vocabulary with long sequences. Merge for a long time and you get a big vocabulary with short ones. GPT-2's config settles at vocab_size: 50257 against a context of n_positions: 1024 tokens.2

    2. Straight from the published GPT-2 config.json — not from memory. Get in the habit: a model's config file is the primary source for its shape.

    Now a detail worth carrying: when GLM-4.6 advertises a 200,000-token context, that is tokens, not characters and not words. How much text it actually holds depends entirely on the tokeniser — and on the language. Text in a language poorly covered by the merge rules tokenises into more pieces, and therefore costs more and remembers less.

    Where the weirdness lives

    The model never sees letters. It sees integers indexing a vocabulary — so low and l,o,w are unrelated symbols to it, sharing nothing. Karpathy's framing, introducing his own tokenizer lecture, is that "a lot of weird behaviors and problems of LLMs actually trace back to tokenization."3 When you next see a model miscount the letters in a word, remember that it was never shown the letters.

    3. Karpathy, Let's build the GPT Tokenizer (2h13m, Feb 2024), which builds minbpe from scratch. He goes further than most: he thinks the stage should ideally be deleted entirely.

    Why not simply use one token per character?

    Where this sits in the pipeline

    You have now built the first stage of the thing you came here to build. Everything downstream consumes its output, and none of it can be changed afterwards — change the vocabulary and every learned parameter that indexes it becomes meaningless.

    Text"lowest"
    →
    Tokeniselow · est_
    →
    Look up IDs[15, 3]
    →
    Model(V,) probs

    Those integers are all the model will ever receive. 15 is not "bigger" than 3 in any meaningful sense — they are names, not quantities. Making the model treat them as meaningful requires giving each one a learned vector, which is called an embedding, and which is the next thing you'll build.

    What you now know

    • Tokenisation is a separate program, trained before the model, with no neural network in it.
    • BPE starts from characters and merges the most frequent pair, repeatedly. It is counting.
    • The merge rules, in order, are the tokeniser. Encoding replays them.
    • Unseen words always encode, by falling back to smaller pieces. No out-of-vocabulary.
    • Vocabulary size is a dial trading sequence length against vocabulary size. GPT-2 chose 50,257.
    • A model never sees letters — which explains a family of its strangest failures.
