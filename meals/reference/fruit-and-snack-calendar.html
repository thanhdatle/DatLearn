Fruit & Snack Calendar

Every lunch and dinner in this house closes with a fruit or sữa chua course, so the dessert slot needs a plan rather than a scramble. Twelve months of seasonality, the safety protocol that is not optional, and the three recipes worth memorising.

Read this first. Every July, read it again.

Nhãn, vải and chôm chôm must be pitted and quartered. Every single time.

A young child's airway is about 10 mm wide. A whole longan, lychee or rambutan is 17–20 mm — round, firm, smooth and compressible. It is a near-perfect airway plug, and the slick surface makes it very hard to dislodge even with back blows.src Vietnamese hospitals report recurring emergencies from hạt nhãn — longan pits — including a 27-month-old resuscitated in an ER, and warn that some children end up in a vegetative state.casesrc These are the classic Vietnamese paediatric choking emergency, and July is the season.

  1. Peel.
  2. Split the flesh in half and remove the pit. Never hand over a peeled-but-intact fruit — children pop the whole thing in, and the pit follows it down.
  3. Quarter it for a 3–4-year-old. At 4–5, halve lengthwise. Never round and whole, at any age in this band.
  4. Seated, on a plate. Never in the car, never walking or running, never while laughing, never in front of a screen.

Grapes follow the same rule — quarter under 4, halve lengthwise at 4–5. Whole grapes are the #3 cause of food-related choking deaths in under-5s, after hot dogs and hard candy.AAPCDC

Also hazardous: hard or unripe chunks of apple, guava, raw carrot (slice thin or grate) · dried fruit (sticky, dense) · whole nuts under 4–5 · hạt na (custard-apple seeds — small, hard, black; de-seed carefully) · hạt mít raw.

And know the first-aid errors. The most common Vietnamese mistake is putting fingers into the child's mouth to fish the object out — this drives it deeper — or holding the child upside down and shaking. Learn back-blows and chest thrusts properly, before you need them.src

The 12-month calendar

A framing caveat that matters. Most Vietnamese seasonal-fruit articles are written from a Hanoi or a Mekong point of view. Đà Nẵng sits in the middle and buys from both. Its wholesale hub, Chợ đầu mối Hòa Cường, aggregates vải from Hưng Yên, bơ from Đắk Lắk, chuối from Gia Lai, nho from Ninh Thuận, thanh long from Bình Thuận and sầu riêng from the south — so the city effectively gets the union of both calendars, at a 1–2 week lag and a small transport premium.src

The always-available baseline

Chuối · đu đủ · thanh long · ổi · dưa hấu · dừa · thơm/dứa · hồng xiêm. Long or year-round harvests, and the cheapest reliable default plate.

The planning rule: build each week around 1–2 in-season "hero" fruits (cheap, peak flavour) plus one year-round staple as backup, so you are never stuck.

Timing, and portion

Fruit is best served ~30–45 min after the meal, or as an afternoon snack — rather than piled straight on top of a full rice meal.src Very acidic fruit (pineapple, unripe mango) should not be given on an empty stomach — which is exactly the pattern you already use.

Portion: ages 4–8 need 1–1.5 cups of fruit a day (USDA MyPlate). Across a two-dessert day, that is roughly ½ cup — a small saucer, ~60–80 g — after lunch and again after dinner.MyPlate Prefer whole fruit to juice. Juice is a rare treat, not a daily serving.

Which fruits to limit — and why "nóng" is the wrong reason

A folk belief, corrected

Vietnam's National Institute of Nutrition is blunt about it: there are no physiologically "hot" (nóng) fruits. Jackfruit, mango and longan are not "heaty." The real variable is sugar — high sugar raises blood glucose, which creates a friendlier environment for staph bacteria, which produces the spots and boils that folk wisdom blames on heat.src

So: limit, do not ban — and limit for the correct reason. Cap the very sweet, calorie-dense ones: sầu riêng · mít · nhãn · vải · chuối sứ · very ripe sweet xoài. A common Vietnamese guideline for lychee is ~100 g (5–6 fruit) per sitting for a child, and the same restraint applies to longan.

And rotate. Variety beats volume — a saucer with three different fruits beats a bowl of one sweet one.

The sugar budget — the number that decides everything else

Children aged 2–18: under 6 teaspoons ≈ 25 g of added sugar per day (AAP / AHA).AAP For perspective: a single tub of sweetened yogurt can eat a meaningful slice of that. A sweetened yogurt at lunch, plus a chè at dinner, plus a slice of full-sugar banana bread at breakfast, blows the limit before anything else is counted.

The design rule, therefore: default to unsweetened yogurt + fruit for the everyday dessert course, and spend the sugar budget deliberately — on the one or two chè a week, and on the halved-sugar banana cake.

Fruit's own sugar is NOT "added sugar" and does not count against the 25 g. Which is exactly why the fruit-plate habit is the right backbone — and why it costs you nothing.

Homemade sữa chua is worth the trouble for one reason: you control the sugar. The standard Vietnamese recipe is 2 L unsweetened milk + 1 can condensed milk + 1 tub of plain yogurt as starter; heat, cool to 37–45 °C (hotter kills the culture), whisk in the starter, incubate 6–8 h — a rice cooker on "warm" works. Halve the condensed milk. It still sets and it still tastes good. Or go fully unsweetened and let the fruit do the work.

The three recipes worth memorising

Chia seed pudding

Breakfast · make it Sunday, eat it all week

3 tbsp chia : 1 cup (240 ml) milk

The one ratio to memorise. Thick, but not rubbery. Want it drinkable? Drop to 2 tbsp per cup.src

  1. Combine chia + milk + a pinch of salt. Sweetener optional — let the fruit do it.
  2. Stir well.
  3. Wait 5–10 minutes, then STIR AGAIN, hard. This is the step everyone skips and the one that matters — it breaks the clumps. Skip it and you get a gel raft on top and watery seeds underneath.
  4. Cover, refrigerate. Minimum 2 h; overnight is best.
  5. Keeps ~5 days. Portion into small glasses; top fresh each morning.

Kid note: chia is high-fibre. A 3–6-year-old gets ~⅓ of an adult portion, and it must be fully hydrated and set — never dry or under-soaked. The overnight method guarantees this.

July toppings: diced mango · longan (pitted, quartered) · măng cụt segments · banana coins · dragon fruit · jackfruit strips · a spoon of coconut milk.

Bánh chuối nướng

The Vietnamese one · dense, sticky, coconut-and-bread

Not a loaf. It is a pudding-like cake — banana slices bound with stale bread soaked in sweetened coconut milk and egg, baked long and slow. This is what most Vietnamese people mean by bánh chuối nướng.src

  1. Bananas ripe but NOT mushy — they must hold their shape. Chuối sứ traditionally; Cavendish works.
  2. Soak the peeled bananas in salt water 15–30 min. A genuinely Vietnamese trick: it prevents browning and gives the cake its characteristic reddish tone.
  3. Slice ~2 cm thick — not thin. Cook briefly in rum + salt (or skip the rum — vanilla is fine with a small child).
  4. Cube stale bread; soak in the sweetened coconut-milk + egg mixture. Fold in the bananas.
  5. Cover with foil, bake ~90 min at 150 °C. Uncover, raise to 180 °C, brush with honey + butter, 15 min more for a golden top.
  6. Cool completely in the pan before unmolding. It needs to set.

Keeps: 2–3 days at room temp; 3–4 days chilled (it firms as the coconut milk sets — many prefer it cold); freezes 1 month. Store in a container, not plastic wrap — wrapping makes the banana slices sweat.

The air fryer works for this, and is much faster. You have one — use it.

Banana bread

The loaf · King Arthur · halve the sugar

The highest-trust recipe in this workspace — professionally tested, gram-accurate.src 165 °C, 9"×5" loaf pan.

  1. Stir butter + sugar + vanilla + cinnamon + nutmeg + raising agents + salt vigorously for 2–3 min. Not optional — this is where the texture comes from.
  2. Add mashed banana, jam and honey (the signature move — they supply the moisture) and the eggs. Banana lumps are fine.
  3. Add the flour. Omit the walnuts for a young child — whole nuts are a choking hazard — or grind them fine.
  4. Rest the batter in the pan for 10 min.
  5. Bake 45 min, tent with foil, bake 25 min more. A few wet crumbs on the toothpick; no raw batter.

CUT THE SUGAR — King Arthur explicitly blesses this. Halve the brown sugar (to ⅓ cup), or go all the way down to 2 tablespoons. Both versions stay moist; they simply taste less sweet and more banana-y. Do this. It is the single best change for a young child, and it buys back a large slice of the 25 g budget.

Freezes 3 months. Double the batch and freeze one loaf — a slice thawed on a weekday morning is precisely the light breakfast this family wants.

Suggested cadence

Fruit plate — ~5 dinners a week. The backbone.
Sữa chua (plain, or with fruit) — ~4–5 lunches a week.
Chè — 1–2× a week, weekend-leaning. Chè chuối is the easiest and the most kid-friendly, and it uses up over-ripe bananas — which also feeds the banana-cake habit. Cut every published chè recipe's sugar in half on the first attempt; the coconut milk and the fruit carry it.
Chia pudding / bánh chuối — breakfast, not dessert. Keep them there.
