# Cơm Nhà — teaching notes

## Learner

- **Dat — the cook.** Not a spectator, not the person being cooked for. Every lesson lands as
  *"here is what you do at the stove,"* never *"here is what is nutritious."* He is the one standing
  in the kitchen at 5:40pm.
- **The table:** Dat, his wife, and one child aged **3–6**.
- **Đà Nẵng.** Coastal, seafood-cheap, Central-Vietnamese palate (salt-forward, chilli-forward,
  mắm ruốc / mắm nêm as umami engines). Đà Nẵng buys from **both** the Northern and Southern fruit
  calendars via Chợ đầu mối Hòa Cường — so seasonality here is the *union*, not the Hanoi list.
- **It is mid-July 2026** — the peak fruit month of the Vietnamese year. Lessons written now should
  say so and use it.

## The constraint scope — CONFIRMED with the user, do not widen it

This is the single most important line in this file, because **the research dossiers get it wrong**
and a future session that trusts them will over-restrict the menu.

> **Banned: `thịt kho tàu` / `thịt kho trứng` / `thịt kho tiêu` — the caramel-braised pork dish
> itself, and only that.**
>
> **ALLOWED — confirmed directly by the user:** `tôm rim` · `sườn rim` · `sườn xào chua ngọt` ·
> `gà kho` · **`cá kho`**.

The meal-structure dossier (§2.0) assumed the ban extended to the whole *rim / khìa / kho* family of
pork braises and greyed them out. **That assumption is overridden.** The dislike is a dislike of
**one dish**, not of a technique. `kho` and `rim` are fully live tools on every other protein — and
`cá kho` in particular is not merely permitted but *canonical* (see below).

**Teach the constraint as a feature, not a loss.** Vietnamese cultural scholarship (Trần Quốc Vượng,
Trần Ngọc Thêm; peer-reviewed in Marinescu & Le 2022) reduces the traditional meal to
**cơm – rau – cá**: rice, vegetables, **fish**. The canonical Vietnamese protein was never braised
pork. A menu built on `cá kho` / `cá sốt cà` / `cá chiên` / `canh cá` is **more traditional, not
less**. Lead with that framing every time it comes up. It is true, it is cited, and it dissolves the
anxiety entirely.

## Equipment — confirmed

Rice cooker · **electric kettle** · **air fryer** · **pressure cooker** · **oven**.

Lessons and timelines may lean on all five. Consequences worth exploiting:

- **Electric kettle** — the highest-ROI item in the kitchen. Boils ~3× faster than a pot on the hob;
  saves 5–7 minutes of pure dead waiting *per meal*. Every timeline should start it at T+0.
- **Air fryer** — turns "marinated protein" into walk-away cooking. Frees the cook's attention
  entirely, which is the scarce resource under the one-active-dish rule.
- **Pressure cooker** — a *weekend* tool (bone broth 2 h → 30 min). Do not put it on a weeknight
  critical path.
- **Oven** — unlocks bánh chuối nướng and banana bread as real breakfast options rather than
  aspirations, and it is why "baking, but only these two things" survives in the mission scope.

## Time budget

**45–60 min total, weekdays, door-to-table. Weekend prep sessions are explicitly OK.**

The research is unambiguous and the course must say it plainly: **a 3-dish mâm cơm is a 30–40 minute
job, not a 90-minute job — but only if the prep is front-loaded and the stove work is parallelised.**
The 45–60 minutes is not the problem. **Unprepped ingredients are the problem.** Weekend batch prep
is not a nice-to-have; it is the mechanism that makes the weekday number close.

## The kid layer — never optional, threads through every lesson

- **Salt at ~50% of adult seasoning** (PGS.TS Nguyễn Thị Lâm; corroborated by WHO's energy-scaled
  ceiling → ~3 g/day for a 3–5-year-old). The heuristic to repeat until it is reflex:
  *"If it tastes right to you, it is already too salty for the child."*
- **Do NOT cook two meals.** Cook the base dish **under-seasoned**, and let the adults add salt at the
  table. The Vietnamese meal format is *built* for this — the nước chấm bowl **is** a per-person
  seasoning dial. This is the design insight the whole kid layer rests on.
- **Chilli on the table, never in the pot.** Structurally lucky: Central VN already does this (ớt nhồi
  floating on the bún chả cá; a saucer of ớt xanh). It means almost every dish is kid-adaptable
  **by omission**, not by re-cooking.
- **Mắm nêm** is the one real blocker, because in bún mắm nêm it is poured *into* the bowl rather than
  served beside it. Three ladders: (1) substitute nước mắm chua ngọt in the kid's own bowl;
  (2) soften — use the *fried* mắm nêm prep, which cuts the raw-fermented smell kids object to;
  (3) ladder up one drop at a time over months. **Taste for mắm nêm is learned. There is no rush.**
- **The choking protocol is non-negotiable and must be visually prominent wherever fruit appears.**
  Nhãn / vải / chôm chôm: **peel → split → remove the pit → quarter.** Never hand over a
  peeled-but-intact fruit; the child pops it in whole and the pit follows. Quarter grapes under 4.
  These are *the* classic Vietnamese paediatric choking emergency, with real ER cases — not a
  theoretical risk during a July fruit season.
- **Dedicated kid protein.** A dietitian reviewing a real family-of-three tray found the fish dish was
  correctly sized *for the tray* but left the **child short once split three ways** — because adults
  moderate their own protein and nobody actively checks the child's. Fix: fry an extra egg, boil a few
  quail eggs. Don't rely on their share of the communal plate.
- **Cap the meal at 30 minutes** (Bệnh viện Nhi Trung ương), even if they ate little. No screens. No
  force-feeding — coercion triggers stress hormones that *suppress gastric secretion*, so the child
  literally digests less. Never food-as-reward. Refused a new food? Offer it again another day.
- **< 25 g added sugar/day** (AAP/AHA). Fruit's own sugar does not count — which is exactly why the
  fruit plate is the right default and the sweetened yogurt tub is not.

## Teaching stance (locked)

**The spine of this topic is structure, not recipes.** He does not need more recipes; the internet has
ten thousand. He needs the **grid** — so that at 5:40pm on a Tuesday the question is not *"what do I
cook?"* but *"which fish, and which vegetable?"* That is a two-second question, and it is the whole
point.

Three frames, in the order they matter:

1. **The mâm cơm is a slot grid, not a recipe list.** Four slots — *mặn / rau / canh / cơm* (+ tráng
   miệng). You do not choose a *meal*; you fill *slots*. **Lesson 1.**
2. **One active dish.** The quán cơm never cooks three attention-hungry dishes. It is always
   *one dish that needs you + two that are basically passive* (a boil, a 4-minute stir-fry, a soup
   that simmers itself). **Two active dishes will not land in 45 minutes** — the single most useful
   predictive rule in the course, and it belongs in lesson 1 alongside the grid.
3. **The two-axis rotation** — protein (`cá → heo → gà → tôm/mực → bò → trứng/đậu`) × technique
   (`kho / xào / hấp / nướng / luộc / chiên / canh`). Same protein, different technique = a different
   meal. This is the mechanism that produces 7 days of variety from 5 proteins.

⚠️ **The rotation is INFERENCE, not a cited fact.** No source states it as an algorithm; it is
synthesised from how sources consistently *describe* the practice. Label it honestly wherever it
appears. Do not launder a good frame into a false citation.

## The highest-leverage technique in the whole course

**The blanching water IS the canh.** Boil the vegetables → lift them out → that water, sharpened with
tomato/lime and a little protein, is a legitimate soup. **Two slots, one pot, near-zero extra time.**

It ships with its caveats **every single time**, because the SEO blogs drop them and this course does
not:

- Use it **immediately**. Don't keep it overnight; don't re-boil it.
- Only with vegetables of **known provenance**, washed well.
- Add the acid **after** you lift the greens out, or they yellow. Don't squeeze lime into
  scalding-hot broth — it turns bitter.
- The "acid destroys the nutrients" rumour is **false** (PGS.TS Nguyễn Duy Thịnh): the colour change
  is chlorophyll reacting to citric acid, not nutrient loss.

The same logic extends to **gà luộc** and **thịt luộc** — the poaching liquid becomes the canh stock.
One pot, two dishes.

## Lesson roadmap

Lesson 1 is live (**the slot grid + the one-active-dish rule**). The backlog, ranked by how much
weeknight pain each removes:

2. **The timeline drill.** T+0 → T+30, walked minute by minute, twice, with the family's actual
   equipment. Rice cooker first; kettle on immediately; mise en place *before* the stove; blanch →
   lift → canh; **stir-fry LAST** (món xào dies fastest and must hit the table hot). The drill *is*
   the lesson — make him run it, not read it.
3. **Sunday, 20 minutes: plan the week.** The rotation as a filled grid; the shopping list falling out
   of it; what to thaw and when. *"Quên rã đông là kẻ thù của việc nấu nhanh."* Safe thawing: cold
   running water or the microwave's defrost setting — **never hot water**.
4. **Batch-prep Sunday.** Process the shopping *immediately* on getting home, not "later" — the
   discipline that makes or breaks the system. Cut to final bite size, **pre-marinate**, portion,
   freeze. Marinated meat freezes *better*. Pre-mix a sauce jar. Batch the slow dish for two meals
   (cá kho genuinely tastes better on day two).
5. **The mắm nêm ladder** — and the wider "adults season at the table" principle. This is the lesson
   that lets the family eat *Đà Nẵng* food rather than a defanged version of it.
6. **Seasonal fruit & the dessert course.** The pairing rule (heavy/oily main → acidic fruit, to
   *cắt cơn ngấy*; light main → richer dessert), the sugar budget, and sữa chua trái cây as the move
   that merges two courses and lets you buy **unsweetened** yogurt.
7. **Restaurant → home adaptations.** Go eat the benchmark, then rebuild the *structure*. Start with
   the three easiest wins: **bánh tráng cuốn thịt heo** (the only cooking is boiling pork),
   **cơm gà Hội An** (one pot; the poaching liquid becomes the canh), **bún chả cá** (buy the chả cá —
   a legitimate shortcut, not a cheat).
8. **Picky eating (biếng ăn).** Behavioural, not nutritional. The 30-minute cap, agency, no screens,
   repeat exposure. Teach only when it becomes a live problem — do not pre-load anxiety.

  `tráng miệng`, `sườn xào chua ngọt`. **Stacked diacritics are the typographic problem of this
  topic**, and a face that handles them is the answer. Body prose stays Newsreader; UI stays Public

- **Reference docs go sans-serif.** Same rule the other topics use: lessons are read once, slowly, at
  a desk; **references are read mid-cook, on a phone, with a pan going.**

## Where to go next

**The timeline drill is lesson 2** and it is the highest-value thing left — the grid tells him *what*
to cook; the timeline is what makes it *land inside the hour*. Those two together are the entire
mission. Everything after is refinement.

Do **not** drift into recipe collection. He does not need more dishes. He needs the structure that
makes the dishes he already knows fire on a Tuesday without thinking.
