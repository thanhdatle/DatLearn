# Mission: Cơm Nhà — Weekday Family Meals in Đà Nẵng

## Why

Put a proper **mâm cơm** on the table every weeknight — *món mặn + rau + canh + cơm*, plus a
fruit or **sữa chua** course — in **45–60 minutes door-to-table**, without the daily
*"hôm nay ăn gì?"* decision spiral and without a 90-minute cook.

The learner is the **cook** (husband), feeding a wife and a child aged **3–6**. The failure mode is
not ignorance of recipes — the internet has ten thousand recipes. The failure mode is **decision
fatigue at 5pm and a timeline that doesn't close.** So the target is not "learn to cook Vietnamese
food." It is: make the three-dish dinner **automatic** — a slot grid you fill, not a menu you agonise
over — and make it land inside the hour, every time, with food a five-year-old will actually eat.

## Success looks like

- **Plan a full week in ≤ 20 minutes** on a Sunday: seven dinners, each a filled slot grid, with the
  shopping list falling out of it.
- **A 3-dish dinner on the table in ≤ 60 minutes** on a normal Tuesday, cooked alone, from a
  standing start — and knowing *before* starting which single dish is the one that needs attention.
- Never cook **two attention-hungry dishes** on the same weeknight. Recognise the trap on sight.
- **Rotate without repeating**: name tomorrow's protein and technique without opening anything,
  because the two-axis rotation is internalised.
- **Feed the kid safely and adequately** from the shared tray: seasoning at ~50% adult salt, chilli
  on the table not in the pot, fruit prepped to the choking protocol (nhãn/vải/chôm chôm **pitted
  and quartered**, every time), and a dedicated protein when the shared dish won't stretch.
- **Buy what's actually in season** — know that mid-July means chôm chôm, măng cụt, and the opening
  of nhãn, and that this is the cheapest, best fruit month of the year.
- Eat at a benchmark quán, then **reproduce the *structure* at home** — not a copy, an adaptation.

## Constraints

- **Time: 45–60 minutes total on weekdays**, door-to-table. **Weekend batch prep is allowed and
  encouraged** — it is in fact the mechanism that makes the weekday number achievable.
- **Every dinner ≥ 3 dishes.** The floor is *món mặn + rau (xào or luộc) + canh + cơm*. This is not
  an aspiration; it is the standard structure of a Vietnamese home meal.
- **Every lunch and dinner ends with a snack/dessert course** — seasonal fruit, sữa chua, or
  occasionally chè.
- **No thịt kho.** Narrowly: **thịt kho tàu / thịt kho trứng / thịt kho tiêu** — the caramel-braised
  pork dish itself. **Close relatives are allowed and welcome**: `tôm rim`, `sườn rim`,
  `sườn xào chua ngọt`, `gà kho`, and **`cá kho`**. This is a *narrow* dislike of one dish, not a
  ban on the kho technique.
- **Kitchen:** rice cooker, electric kettle, **air fryer**, **pressure cooker**, and an **oven**.
  Timelines may lean on all five.
- **The kid is 3–6.** Salt at ~50% of adult seasoning; chilli always on the table, never in the pot;
  mắm nêm needs a workaround, not an exclusion; the fruit choking protocol is non-negotiable;
  < 25 g added sugar/day.
- **Location: Đà Nẵng.** Coastal, seafood-cheap, Central-Vietnamese palate. The local restaurant
  scene is the palate calibrator and the source of ideas.

## Out of scope

- **Weight-loss dieting, macro tracking, calorie counting.** This is about feeding a family well,
  not about a body-composition goal.
- **Entertaining and party food.** Feasts, Tết trays, dinner parties — different problem, different
  timeline, different lessons.
- **Baking beyond the two breakfast items.** Bánh chuối nướng and banana bread are in scope because
  the family actually eats them for breakfast. Bread, pastry, cake decorating: no.
- **Dishes the restaurant does better than any home kitchen can.** Cao lầu is physically
  unreproducible outside Hội An (the noodle needs Cù Lao Chàm lye water and Bá Lễ well water); ốc hút
  is a chilli-bomb that takes an hour to *eat*. These are "go out and order it" dishes, and this
  course says so rather than pretending otherwise.
- **Authenticity policing.** A shortcut that gets a real dinner on a real table beats a pure recipe
  that never gets cooked.
